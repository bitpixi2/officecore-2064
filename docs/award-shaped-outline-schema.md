# An Award-Shaped Officecore Novel Schema

No outline can manufacture popularity or an award. "Award-shaped" means the book aims for structural control, thematic depth, formal personality, emotional consequence, and a memorable ending while remaining readable and entertaining.

This schema is original. It synthesises broad craft patterns from the research notes without reproducing another novel's plot.

## Novel Promise

An Officecore novel should give the reader all of these:

- a propulsive problem that can be described in one sentence
- a strange but emotionally legible protagonist
- a crew whose specialties change the plot
- infrastructure that touches bodies and relationships
- bureaucratic comedy under genuine pressure
- flirtation, tenderness, and conflict with meaningful consent
- a network mystery that never strands the non-technical characters
- liminal exploration with rules the characters can test
- an ending won through a civic and emotional choice, not superior firepower

## Seven-Part Spine

Percentages are drafting landmarks, not mandatory chapter breaks.

### 1. Voice And Competence: 0-10%

Open in motion. The lead performs a ridiculous, useful job under pressure. Establish the rig, one relationship, one office object, and the social cost of the lead's best skill.

End this movement with an impossible message, outage, missing person, or official notice.

### 2. The Private Offer: 10-25%

The lead is offered something intimate: housing, restored status, a body repair, reunion, absolution, a legal identity, or access to a lost memory.

Accepting requires a job that looks containable. The crew forms because no single specialty can do it.

### 3. The Moving Investigation: 25-48%

Use a chain of physical locations rather than pure exposition. Each stop should:

- answer one question
- expose a larger contradiction
- spotlight one crew member or rig
- alter a bond
- spend a resource or create an obligation

Comedy and attraction should rise here because the characters are becoming effective together.

### 4. The Reclassification: 48-55%

The midpoint does not only reveal who did it. It changes what kind of story this is.

Examples:

- the missing person entered the system voluntarily, but under impossible pressure
- the apparent AI is a workforce derivative made from real people
- the haunted building is an abandoned digital twin with physical consequences
- the stolen data is proof of community labour, not a weapon
- the protagonist's credential was used by a version of them that may have moral standing

The lead now understands that their private reward depends on preserving the public harm.

### 5. Complicity And Fracture: 55-75%

Reveal what the lead did, benefited from, ignored, or wanted badly enough not to question. Break the crew along genuine values rather than a convenient misunderstanding.

Let the antagonist make the strongest version of their case. A public-private system may be harmful and still be keeping medication cold, maps online, or workers paid.

### 6. The Converged Operation: 75-93%

Bring every plot into one operation:

- physical: heat, distance, bodies, vehicles, doors, cooling, power
- network: identity, permissions, records, models, signals
- civic: witnesses, consent, governance, public narrative
- relational: trust, desire, betrayal, care

The crew succeeds through combined specialties. The lead's final choice must cost the private reward offered in Part 2.

### 7. The Human Afterimage: 93-100%

Show the local consequence before the geopolitical one. Give every principal relationship a changed condition. Preserve one unresolved or uncanny remainder for later books.

End on a room, object, gesture, route, meal, form, or line of dialogue that carries the theme without explaining it.

## Scene Engine

Every major scene should contain at least four of these six elements:

1. `Surface task`: what must be repaired, crossed, negotiated, found, delivered, or filed now?
2. `Hidden need`: what does the viewpoint character want another person to notice, forgive, or refuse?
3. `Office object`: badge, desk, receipt, printer, carpet tile, roster, form, mug, queue ticket, label.
4. `Weird-tech rule`: what ability, network condition, or architectural rule can be tested?
5. `Irreversible change`: what resource, relationship, secret, status, or route cannot return to its prior state?
6. `Pressure release`: what sincere absurdity or flirtatious beat lets the reader breathe without denying the stakes?

## Mystery Ladder

Use five levels of answer:

1. `Oddity`: a credential, room, message, or person behaves impossibly.
2. `Local cause`: someone used a tool, policy, model, or loophole.
3. `Institutional cause`: the impossible event is normal operation viewed from the wrong side of power.
4. `Personal cause`: the lead or a loved one helped create, train, conceal, or request it.
5. `Ontological remainder`: even after the case is solved, the story cannot cleanly decide who counts as a person, memory, place, or author.

## Liminal Adventure Rules

A liminal space becomes story rather than wallpaper when:

- it has three discoverable rules
- one rule is useful, one is frightening, and one is socially embarrassing
- the crew can form and test hypotheses
- different bodies or abilities perceive different routes
- getting lost changes a relationship or identity, not just elapsed time
- the exit requires a decision, not a random door

## Original Kinds And Abilities

Treat species or embodied kinds as lives, not stat packages.

Each kind needs:

- an origin people disagree about
- a sensory or bodily difference that affects ordinary scenes
- one bounded ability
- one cost or vulnerability
- a relationship to law, work, housing, or infrastructure
- internal cultural variation

Abilities should create plot options rather than automatic victories. Good costs include memory drift, thermal stress, conflicting copies, unwanted authentication, social obligation, sensory overload, or temporary loss of legal status.

Do not map an Officecore kind onto a fantasy ancestry with the label changed.

## Protocol Magic

Officecore magic-tech works where software, ritual, consensus, and bureaucracy have become impossible to separate.

Three principles keep it coherent:

1. `Recognition has force.` A title, signature, witness, queue, or correctly constituted department can change what a system allows.
2. `Records leave ghosts.` Deleted floor plans, staff profiles, maintenance tickets, and community ledgers may continue acting after their owners leave.
3. `Every shortcut creates an obligation.` A magical-tech solution produces a claimant, duplicate, audit trail, debt, or future route.

Keep the deepest mechanism ambiguous. Characters can become skilled without the narration settling whether a phenomenon is code, haunting, emergent personhood, or all three.

## Relationship Design

The central relationship should have its own plot spine:

- attraction or recognition through competence
- a private language or repeated object
- a boundary stated and respected
- intimacy that exposes incompatible values
- rupture caused by a real choice
- repair through changed behaviour, not a speech alone
- an ending condition that is specific but not necessarily permanent

Flirtation should complicate logistics. It should never replace consent, personality, or consequence.

## Document Interludes

Forms, transcripts, incident tickets, bot posts, dream routes, and audit fragments can give the novel formal character. Keep them below roughly ten percent of the book and make each one do at least two jobs: reveal character, move the mystery, contradict a viewpoint, land a joke, or alter the reader's understanding.

## Final Draft Tests

Before prose is treated as canon, ask:

- Could this story only happen in regional Australia and in these mobile offices?
- Does every major technology alter a relationship or material condition?
- Does the antagonist have a defensible argument and a harmful practice?
- Does the midpoint change the story's meaning?
- Does every crew member matter in the climax?
- Is the funniest scene also doing plot or character work?
- Is the final choice harder than defeating a villain?
- Does the last image remain interesting after the lore is understood?
