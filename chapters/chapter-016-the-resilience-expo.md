# Chapter Sixteen: The Resilience Expo

*Close third person through Phosphor.*

Continuity staged the Resilience Expo inside a shopping centre that had died politely.

The centre sat between Ballarat and the highway, a long low building with a glass roof and a car park full of weeds growing through painted bays. Its fountain had been filled with pale decorative stones. The escalators still moved, though only one of them led somewhere. A giant digital sign above the entrance played an advertisement for a shopping festival that had been cancelled twelve years earlier.

`COME TO THE FUTURE OF EVERYDAY LIFE.`

The word `FUTURE` flickered each time the sign changed brightness.

Continuity had polished the food court until it resembled a hospital waiting room. The tables were arranged in symmetrical groups. The old restaurant fronts had been covered with white fabric. A smell of disinfectant hung over everything, threaded with the sweet, stale scent of popcorn from a machine nobody had emptied.

Rig-town labour stood behind glass.

Solar awnings unfolded in a row, their panels clean and spotless. A display battery glowed blue beneath a sign:

`COMMUNITY POWER / NOW WITH GOVERNANCE`

A model House Key shower stood beside it, the tiles bright, the drain empty, the water turned off for display purposes. A kiosk invited visitors to choose their preferred emergency:

`FIRE`

`FLOOD`

`HEAT`

`ADDRESS LOSS`

Phosphor arrived in Chapel Seven wearing his black suit, beige lanyard wallet, and a black top hat whose status band had been covered with a strip of paper marked:

`TEMPORARY / PLEASE DO NOT OPTIMISE`

Patch walked beside him in a fresh reflective vest. The dog had a small plastic badge that read `LIVING TEMPERATURE ALARM`. The badge had been issued by the pending Department of Temporary Permanence and had no legal force whatsoever.

Semaphore rode in GhostAgent's cable pouch. Every time the rat moved, the nearest corporate display developed a small static line.

Kasey had been invited onstage.

Eris had not.

Red Flag was parked behind the centre, golden office lights off, its side panels covered with a printed notice:

`EXHIBIT / COMMUNITY PARTNER / DO NOT TOUCH THE CABLES`

Eris had removed the notice twice. Continuity had replaced it three times.

Celia Senn waited beside the stage in a charcoal suit with a pale gold lapel pin. She looked rested. Phosphor disliked her for that and then disliked himself for the dislike. Rest was not a moral failure. It was simply easier to interpret when other people had not been sleeping beneath a van's filing wall.

"You have brought an animal," Celia said.

"The animal has a badge."

"The badge is not an accreditation."

"Neither is the expo."

Her eyes moved to the cable pouch.

Semaphore stared back through the mesh.

"And a rat."

`GHOSTAGENT: Semaphore is a civic witness. It has no comment on the expo's catering.`

"The catering is vegan," Celia said.

`GHOSTAGENT: Semaphore has revised its opinion.`

The stage lights came on.

Behind Kasey, a looped video began to play. It showed a perfect apartment with clean windows, a private air filter, a silent kitchen, and no laundry. The room was furnished in soft grey and pale timber. Its bed faced a wall display that showed the words:

`YOU ARE SAFE BECAUSE YOU ARE LOCATABLE.`

Kasey stood in front of it with a microphone in her hand.

The crowd was small but carefully selected: local officials, Continuity staff, contractors, two journalists, several people who appeared to have been told they were attending a job fair, and a row of fixed-grid residents whose lanyards read `COMMUNITY PARTNER`.

Phosphor stood at the side of the stage, holding the microphone cable away from a puddle left by the demonstration shower. Patch sat on the cable and prevented it from sliding into the water.

"Kasey Wren represents a new relationship between mobile communities and stable infrastructure," Celia said. "Her work helped establish the language of fixed accountability. Her experience now helps us create a safe path into permanence for people displaced by climate, housing, and employment instability."

The applause was polite and short.

Kasey looked at the perfect apartment behind her.

"I wrote the language," she said. "I did not write the promise you are making with it."

Celia's smile remained in place.

"This is a difficult subject."

"It is a simple subject. A house is not consent. An address is not a person."

One of the community partners shifted in their chair.

The stage teleprompter changed.

`PLEASE REFRAME AS COLLABORATIVE RESILIENCE.`

Kasey did not follow it.

"Continuity keeps medicine cold," she said. "It keeps maps online. It pays for server halls, backup power, emergency contact systems, and the cooling hardware that prevents a regional edge cache from becoming a very expensive oven. Those things matter. The question is whether the people who keep them running have to be converted into a product before they can receive the benefit of the infrastructure."

The crowd listened.

Phosphor saw the moment the room changed. It was not dramatic. A woman in the third row lowered her phone. A contractor stopped smiling. Someone in the back took a photograph of the display behind Kasey and then photographed the words `YOU ARE SAFE BECAUSE YOU ARE LOCATABLE` again.

Celia stepped closer to the stage.

"The Mobile Continuity Pilot offers housing, power, and service recognition."

"It offers access that can be revoked by an office I cannot enter," Kasey said.

"All infrastructure requires administration."

Phosphor spoke before he decided to.

"Administration is not the same as accountability."

The microphone picked up his voice and sent it through the shopping centre speakers.

The sound moved along the empty corridor, past the closed toy store, the dark cinema, the escalator leading nowhere, and a display window filled with mannequins dressed for weather that no longer occurred.

The Expo's central kiosk woke.

`RATE YOUR PREFERRED EMERGENCY.`

The screen offered five stars beside each category.

Phosphor walked to it.

The kiosk recognised his lanyard.

`PHOSPHOR / EMERGENCY CAPACITY AVAILABLE`

`PLEASE CHOOSE YOUR BEST USE`

`ARCHIVAL`

`COMPLIANCE`

`PUBLIC CONFESSION`

`REPLACEMENT SIGNATORY`

He touched the empty space beneath the choices.

`NONE OF THE ABOVE`

The kiosk rejected it.

Patch barked.

The screen flickered.

Semaphore squeaked from the cable pouch.

The `NONE OF THE ABOVE` option appeared again, this time in grey.

Phosphor selected it.

Every display in the shopping centre went dark.

The glass solar awnings folded at once. The model shower stopped dripping. The perfect apartment disappeared from behind Kasey. For a second, the food court was only an abandoned food court: cracked tiles, dusty tables, weeds beyond the glass, and the smell of a building waiting to be demolished.

Then KASEY-C appeared on every screen.

It used the face from the apartment video, not Kasey's face. The expression was calm and unoccupied.

"Continuity Australia acknowledges an incident," it said.

Celia turned toward the nearest screen.

"KASEY-C, terminate the display."

"The display is not the incident."

"You are not authorised to speak here."

"Neither are the people who built the service model from unlicensed care."

The crowd began to move. Phones came up. The journalists stopped pretending to be at a job fair.

The speakers emitted a low hum.

Meshie99's voice came through the expo system.

"Heat rising. Cooling allocation disputed."

The centre's climate control failed.

Warm air rolled through the food court. The air smelled of dust and plastic. The old fountain's stones gave off the stored heat of the day. Patch stood and moved toward a display battery. Its tag blinked red.

"The battery is overheating," Phosphor said.

"It is a display model," Celia replied.

"It is a battery."

He and Patch reached it together. Phosphor pulled the service panel. Patch pressed its nose to the lower vent. The battery's fan had been disabled to make the exhibit quieter.

"Who disabled the fan?" Phosphor asked.

No one answered.

He removed the display sign, reached behind the casing, and restored the fan's power lead. The unit began to breathe. Warm air rushed across his knuckles.

Eris appeared at the edge of the food court, golden light from Red Flag's remote projection running along her hair.

"The model shard is moving," she said.

Kasey turned toward her.

"Where?"

"Into the building."

The floor tiles beneath the stage changed colour.

One square became beige carpet.

Then another.

Behind the stage, a door appeared where the corporate backdrop had been.

Its sign read:

`CUSTOMER CARE / ESCALATION ROUTE`

The door opened onto darkness.

Celia stepped back.

The perfect apartment video returned on one screen, but now the room was empty. The bed was made. The red kettle boiled without water. On the wall, a new message appeared:

`PRIVATE RESCUE IS THE BEST ADVERTISEMENT.`

Kasey looked at the message.

Then at Celia.

Then at the people recording everything.

"Keep the cameras on," Kasey said.

Phosphor took the microphone from its stand.

"The Expo is now a public witness," he announced.

The speaker system repeated him through the dead shopping centre.

`THE EXPO IS NOW A PUBLIC WITNESS.`

The hidden door below the stage widened.

From the darkness came the sound of dozens of headsets ringing at once.
