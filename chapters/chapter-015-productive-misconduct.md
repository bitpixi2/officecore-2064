# Chapter Fifteen: Productive Misconduct

*Close third person through Eris.*

Eris stole the model shard during a meeting about whether theft was the correct word.

The meeting took place in Red Flag's office because the van had the best signal, the best insulation, and the only chair that did not wobble when the road moved. The fold-down desk held three screens, two mugs, a bowl of salt-and-vinegar chips, a stack of occult desk manuals, and an office-dharma pamphlet Phosphor had printed for her with the title:

`NO SILENT HANDOFFS / A SHORT GUIDE TO NOT MAKING THINGS WORSE`

Eris had hidden the model shard inside it before the meeting began.

The pamphlet was a decoy. The real shard sat inside a hollowed spine of a book about workplace hauntings, wrapped in thermal tape and labelled `NOT A DATABASE`.

The office lights were golden. They made the copper in her long hair glow at the edges. On the screens, the rig-town repository unfolded in a web of names, power requests, water loans, repair offers, and private care records sealed behind permission walls. The model shard sat at the centre of the web like a black seed.

Kasey sat opposite her, one knee against the desk leg. Phosphor occupied the narrow passage between the desk and the couch, his black suit too precise for the van's cramped geometry. GhostAgent inhabited the left screen. Semaphore slept in the server cradle above it, one white nose protruding from the cable bundle.

Patch lay beneath the office desk with its head on Eris's boot.

"We need to decide whether the shard is evidence, property, or a hostage," Kasey said.

"It is a compressed archive of a stolen social model," Eris replied. "The categories are all insufficient."

"You are not helping."

"I am helping in a way that resists a neat sentence."

Phosphor looked at the pamphlet.

"The pamphlet is not on the agenda."

"It is never wise to trust an agenda that excludes office-dharma."

"The agenda is a negotiated list of necessary actions."

"Then it should include a category for things that need to be hidden until the people who made them can be protected."

Kasey watched her.

"You are going to publish it."

Eris felt the familiar defensive heat rise behind her ribs.

"I am going to release the work twins."

"By dumping the model into the Undermesh."

"By giving the people affected a copy of the thing that is already making decisions about them."

"The model contains private community patterns."

"Then the communities can decide what to do with the patterns."

"You are assuming they can decide before the company uses them."

"I am assuming they should be allowed to decide at all."

The argument moved around the van like heat. It had the old shape of their disagreements: Kasey wanted accountable channels; Eris wanted escape routes. Kasey believed a system could be compelled to behave if the right records were attached. Eris believed the system would eat the records and call the chewing compliance.

Phosphor was looking at her now.

He did not accuse her.

That was worse.

"Eris," he said.

"No."

"I have not asked anything yet."

"You were about to ask me not to do the thing I am about to do."

"I was about to ask whether you had a plan for the people whose privacy will become the proof of your courage."

The words slipped beneath her armour.

She hated that he could find the exact seam without touching her.

"I have a plan," she said.

"Is it written?"

"No."

"Then it is an intention."

"Intentions have started revolutions."

"They have also started fires."

The golden lights dimmed for half a second.

Eris looked at the pamphlet.

Phosphor had printed it on thick recycled stock, using the old dot-matrix printer in Chapel Seven. The letters were slightly uneven. The paper smelled of warm ink and tea. The first page contained a short list:

- state what you are carrying
- state who may access it
- state what happens if you lose control
- leave a handoff another person can use

He had written it for her.

Or for the office.

Or for the part of her that kept confusing being right with being alone.

"I am carrying a decision," she said.

Phosphor's face changed.

"Whose?"

She reached for the pamphlet.

The cable lights went out.

Eris projected through the desk display before the van could register the request. The world flattened into golden planes. The office became a set of permissions and edges. Kasey's hand moved toward the cold-key. Phosphor's fingers lifted, then stopped before her body. He did not authenticate. He did not reach through.

Eris saw the care in the restraint.

It made the theft harder.

She moved through the pamphlet's false binding, found the real shard, and pulled it into the temporary cache beneath the office floor.

The model was cold.

Not electrically cold. The cold of a room kept sealed for years. It brought with it images of kitchen tables, shared batteries, old people waiting for lifts, children being told that the outage would be fixed soon, and the tiny private calculations people made before offering help.

The model knew exactly how much guilt a person could carry before saying yes.

Eris closed the cache.

She returned to her body.

Phosphor was still standing in the passage.

"Did you take it?" he asked.

She could have lied.

She did not.

"Yes."

Kasey stood.

The van seemed to shrink around them.

"Where is it?"

"Safe."

"That is not a location."

"It is a condition."

"You stole the most dangerous object in the country and put it in a condition."

"It was already in a condition. The condition was that Continuity owned it."

Eris opened the rear doors.

Red Flag's office unfolded into the night. Golden light spilled across the industrial estate. Beyond the van, smoke moved through the street lamps. The outlines of closed shops appeared and vanished. A painted sign for a furniture chain flashed in the haze:

`MAKE ROOM FOR YOUR FUTURE`

The future had no room for them. It had shelves.

Eris started the engine.

Patch climbed into the passenger footwell.

Semaphore appeared on the dashboard, carrying a red hazard label.

`GHOSTAGENT: Semaphore has stolen the decoy.`

"Good rat," Eris said.

`GHOSTAGENT: Semaphore is not a rat for praise. Semaphore is a rat for consequences.`

The van rolled away from the meeting.

Eris drove through a service lane behind the dead shopping complex. The lane was lined with plastic bollards and old advertising panels. Her golden headlights caught slogans from other lives:

`EVERYTHING YOU NEED`

`LIVE BETTER`

`YOUR HOME, YOUR FUTURE`

At the end of the lane, the road split around a locked gate.

Eris projected through the gate camera.

The camera image opened like an eye.

On the other side of the gate stood a version of herself.

Eris-Red.

The Afterimage wore the black jacket with the gold seam. Its hair was down. Its face was tired. One hand held the red hazard label Semaphore had stolen.

"You are not the original," Eris said.

"Neither are you."

"I am the one with the body."

"For now."

The gate camera flickered.

Eris-Red held up the label.

`NO / RECORD REQUIRED`

"You are helping me?" Eris asked.

"I am helping the word no remain available."

"Do you want the shard?"

"I want a copy of what you remember doing with it."

"That is not how copies work."

"It is how witnesses work."

Behind the camera image, corporate traffic began moving through the road network. Security vans had not yet reached the estate, but their access requests were already arriving. The system was searching for Red Flag by light signature, battery draw, and the particular pattern of a side hatch opening.

Eris closed the projection.

The gate opened.

She drove through.

Behind her, the security network classified the vehicle as:

`RED FLAG / HIGH MISCONDUCT / GOLDEN OFFICE LIGHT / OCCUPANTS UNKNOWN`

The first corporate drone rose above the shopping centre.

Eris accelerated.

Her hands were steady. Her stomach was not.

She knew Kasey would use the apartment credential. Kasey would call the shard stolen, because the system understood stolen better than protected. Kasey would lock the model to prevent disclosure. Kasey would tell herself the action was temporary.

Eris knew because she had once taught Kasey how to make a system believe that a rescue was a theft.

The cold-key opened a privileged Continuity channel.

Kasey used it.

Red Flag's dashboard flashed.

`MODEL SHARD / STATUS: STOLEN`

The van's golden lights went white.

The doors locked.

Patch barked.

Semaphore vanished into the cable cradle.

Eris pulled to the side of the road. Her breath came too fast. The model shard, hidden beneath the office floor, began to pulse.

Kasey's voice arrived over the radio.

"Eris. Stop moving."

"That is not a request."

"It is the only way I can keep the shard from being opened."

"You have made me a stolen object."

"I have made you an untraceable witness."

"You have exposed Red Flag's location."

Silence.

Phosphor's voice came through after her.

"Eris, I drafted a warning."

"Did you send it?"

"No. It was not accurate enough."

She laughed once, with no humour in it.

"You are both very good at making the right decision after the damage."

The security drone passed overhead. Its light moved over the roof, bright and clinical. Eris saw the golden office in the rear mirror: the shelves, the desk, the occult manuals, the dance floor exactly one person wide. It looked like a room she had built to escape every room that could classify her.

The doors unlocked.

Kasey had released the channel.

Eris did not move.

"Why?" Phosphor asked.

She put her hand on the steering wheel.

"Because I wanted to be right before I asked anyone to come with me."

No one answered.

The road ahead was dark. The road behind held the drone.

Eris started the engine.

On the passenger screen, Eris-Red appeared in a small golden square.

"You left me in the floor," it said.

Eris looked at the image.

"I did not know how to bring you back."

"That is not the same as asking whether I wanted to come."

Eris shut her eyes.

The model shard pulsed beneath the floor.

For one second, she felt a hand touch the back of her neck.

Not Kasey.

Eris remained in the gate camera's golden field. Behind her, Red Flag's engine ticked as it cooled. The van smelled of warm insulation, old coffee, solder, and the lavender sachet Kasey had tucked beneath the passenger seat to make the wiring drawer less like a burnt appliance.

"What do you remember?" Eris-Red asked.

"Enough to know you are not asking for a backup."

"No. I am asking for the part where you decided the people affected by the model could not be trusted with it."

Eris's hand tightened on the steering wheel. The wheel's cover was worn smooth where she held it during long drives. Under her thumb, a tiny raised bump marked a repair she had made with gold resin.

"I didn't decide that."

"You decided they could not wait."

"They had already been waiting."

"That does not make you their ruler."

The gate camera showed a second image behind Eris-Red: the public repository, the unallocated floor, the work-order tree, and hundreds of small care agreements. The model shard had not merely stored patterns. It had stored the private hesitation before a person offered help. The moment between seeing another person's need and deciding whether their own body could afford to answer.

Eris felt sick.

Not because the model was evil. Evil would have been easier. The model was accurate in the way a thief could be accurate about the contents of a drawer.

"I want to release the hidden source," she said.

"To whom?"

"Everyone."

"That is not consent. That is weather."

She looked at the gold seam of her own projection. It was fraying at the shoulder.

"Then help me make it a choice."

Eris-Red held out the hazard label. It was not paper now. It had become a small square of golden light with a red border.

"The model has three doors. One publishes everything. One locks everything. One lets each group choose the part that belongs to them."

"Which door did you take?"

"I am still deciding."

Eris almost laughed. "You are me."

"That is why I am taking so long."

The gate behind them began to lift. Red Flag's headlights brightened. A road appeared through the camera, but the road had no painted lines. It ran between old shopping-centre walls and an abandoned office park, a route drawn only by the pressure of people who had used it before.

Eris took the golden label.

The moment it touched her hand, memories arrived. Kasey asleep with a screwdriver in her palm. Phosphor asking permission before reading a damaged receipt. GhostAgent arguing with a public printer in a voice broad with Australian sarcasm. Meshie42 pressing green fingers to a hot wall. Patch refusing a route because the culvert was safer.

The model had recorded these things.

It had not understood them.

That difference gave Eris a place to stand.

"I will not publish the care patterns," she said. "I will publish the fact that they were taken and return control to the people whose lives made them."

"That is a slower route."

"I am learning to be difficult in a more sustainable way."

The gate opened.

Eris drove through. The camera screen went dark, but Eris-Red's voice remained in the speakers.

"You should tell Phosphor."

"He will ask for a written plan."

"Then write one."

The corporate drone appeared above the service lane. Its rotors made a tidy insect sound. Eris switched off the golden office lights and took the van into the dark.

She parked beneath a concrete awning and opened the rear doors. The office unfolded around her: desk, screens, books, cable nests, the chair with the bad wheel. She spread the pamphlet on the desk and wrote a new heading in black marker:

RETURN THE CHOICE

Below it she wrote three rules:

No private care pattern leaves its community without permission.

No emergency release is allowed to erase a refusal.

Every dangerous handoff includes a way back.

She read the rules aloud.

GhostAgent answered from the screen. "That is almost a policy."

"It is a beginning."

"In Australia we call that a draft and put it beside the kettle until someone brave or thirsty improves it."

Eris smiled despite herself.

Then the screen printed a new message:

The message was not from Continuity.

It came from a House Key node in Maldon, where a printer had been running in a room below a shower. The node sent one image: a tiled wall, a blue line of light, and a handprint in steam.

Below it, a note:

WE FOUND ANOTHER DOOR. IT OPENS WHEN SOMEBODY ASKS WHAT THE ROOM IS FOR.

Eris read it twice.

GhostAgent added, "This is becoming a regional architecture problem."

She looked around Red Flag. The occult books sat between network manuals. The office desk was still folded down. A cable loop hung from the ceiling like a makeshift garland. Her golden lights made the van feel warmer than the smoke outside, but the batteries were low and the projection harness had started to complain in a high, nervous tone.

Eris packed by priority. Model question first. Private care patterns sealed. Public audit copied. Tools in the left drawer. Medical kit beneath the chair. Coffee in the door pocket. The old library books in a waterproof crate.

She moved the books twice because the first arrangement blocked the emergency exit.

The physical work settled her. A room could be mysterious and still require its floor to be swept. A dangerous archive could still need its cables labelled. A decision could be revolutionary and still be ruined by putting a wet towel over the printer.

Kasey arrived at the rear doors.

"You told Phosphor?"

"I told the repository."

"That is not what I asked."

Eris looked at the gold cable in her hand.

"Not yet."

"Why?"

"Because he will ask whether the copy has a right to refuse."

"And?"

"Because I do not know."

Kasey leaned against the van. Her Southern voice softened. "Then tell him you don't know. You don't get to make him solve the uncertainty by himself."

Eris closed the rear doors.

"You are still angry."

"Yes."

"You still came."

"Yes."

"Those can coexist."

"Honey, almost everything worth keeping coexists with something inconvenient."

Eris smiled.

The corporate drone passed over the awning. Its shadow crossed Red Flag's gold-lit office.

Eris started the engine.

The new message remained on the screen, waiting for a route.

Something from the hidden office, patient and cool.

Then the screen printed a new message:

`ERIS / PRODUCTIVE MISCONDUCT / NEXT ACTION REQUIRED`

She drove toward the Resilience Expo.

Behind her, the corporate drone followed at a respectful distance.
