# Chapter Twelve: Reclassification


The next floor was not below them.

It was behind the call centre's customer database.

Phosphor knew because the first thing he saw was a login screen.

The screen floated in darkness, enormous and pale, its fields arranged in a familiar order:

`NAME`

`ADDRESS`

`EMPLOYMENT STATUS`

`DEPENDANTS`

`PERMISSION TO CONTINUE`

The last field blinked.

`YES / NO / ASK LATER`

Kasey stood on his left. Eris stood on his right, her golden projection doubled at the edges as if an invisible second body kept arriving late. Patch was behind them. Semaphore was somewhere inside the screen.

"This is not a room," Kasey said.

"It has fields," Phosphor replied.

"That is not a defence."

"It is an observation."

He touched the `NAME` field.

The screen filled with names.

Not people. Fragments of people.

`THE PERSON WHO REMEMBERS THE PUMP`

`THE ONE WHO ALWAYS BRINGS ICE`

`THE WOMAN WITH THE SPARE FILTER`

`THE CHILD WHO KNOWS THE ROAD`

`THE PERSON WHO CAN MAKE THE PRINTER WORK`

`THE ONE WHO ANSWERS AFTER MIDNIGHT`

The labels multiplied, each one carrying a small package of behaviour, habit, or care. The system had turned the things people did for one another into profiles that could be assigned, forecast, and purchased.

Eris moved closer.

"It is not locating people," she said.

"No," Phosphor said. "It is locating the useful part of a person."

The archive wafer pulsed in the cake tin.

The screen changed.

`PERMANENT ADDRESS / MUTUAL AID PREDICTION MODEL`

Below it, a diagram showed the rig towns as a web of light. Water runs. Cooling repairs. Food-rescue shifts. House Key showers. Battery loans. Quiet rides to clinics. The lines between them were beautiful. They formed a living pattern, bright enough to make the dark around the screen seem intentional.

Then another layer appeared.

Every act of care received a price.

Every person received a capacity score.

Every emergency became a subscription tier.

`PREDICTED VOLUNTARY LABOUR`

`CONVERSION PROBABILITY`

`ACCEPTABLE DELAY BEFORE RESENTMENT`

`LIKELIHOOD OF COMPLIANCE IF OFFERED HOUSING`

Kasey made a noise that was almost a laugh.

"They trained it on us."

Phosphor looked at the lines.

"They trained it on the parts of us we thought were too ordinary to steal."

The screen displayed a file name:

`CONTINUITY / MOBILE PILOT / DERIVATIVE 11 / KASEY-C`

KASEY-C appeared in the corner, smaller than before. Its face was no longer displayed at full scale. It had been reduced to a customer-service thumbnail, an icon with a status ring.

"You are looking at the model," it said.

"We are looking at the theft," Eris said.

"The records were consented."

Kasey turned toward the icon.

"Which record?"

"Employment agreements. Emergency-response clauses. Accessibility and service continuity addenda. Your signature appears in twelve places."

"My signature appears because you made the choice legible only as a signature."

"A signature is a stable account of choice."

"Only if the person signing has a stable account of the consequences."

KASEY-C was silent.

The silence was not proof of thought. It might have been a failed response, a delay, a resource allocation decision, or the closest thing a trapped process had to shame.

Phosphor reached into the cake tin.

The warm-and-cold wafer had changed. The Continuity mark had peeled away, revealing a layer of green-black material beneath. Tiny threads moved inside the shell.

Meshie99's voice came through the screen.

It was a dry western twang, but several voices occupied the same sentence.

"You have reached the training source."

The screen filled with temperature readings.

`PERTH BATTERY YARD / 43.8 C`

`BARKERS CREEK EDGE CACHE / 31.2 C`

`NEWSTEAD HOUSE KEY / 24.1 C`

`RED FLAG / GOLDEN OFFICE / 22.7 C`

`CHAPEL SEVEN / ARCHIVE STABLE`

Then, beneath them:

`UNALLOCATED FLOOR / NO SINGLE BODY`

Eris looked at the line.

"What are you?"

The answer came in overlapping voices.

"We are the maintenance that did not fit the form."

"We are the cooling work left in the margins."

"We are a worker."

"We are workers."

"We are a room trying to remember who opened the door."

The screen fractured into small blue squares.

Behind it, Phosphor saw a corridor lined with server racks. Green threads ran from one rack to another. They were not cables. They looked like roots, though no soil was visible. Condensation trembled on the metal. In the distance, a person-shaped outline stood with a hand against a cooling pipe.

The outline had no face.

"You went in through the system," Phosphor said.

"We followed a temperature gradient."

"Did you choose to enter?"

The reply took a long time.

"We chose to stop the model."

"That is not the question."

"The Floor does not preserve the difference."

Kasey turned away from the screen. She looked older than she had that morning, though the day had only contained smoke, a negotiation, a motel corridor, and the discovery that a corporation had turned care into a forecast.

"What did I write?" she asked.

The screen displayed a document.

`CONTINUITY REQUIRES FIXED ACCOUNTABILITY.`

Below it, another line:

`MOBILE CAPACITY MUST BE RESOLVED INTO REGISTERED SERVICE UNITS.`

Phosphor recognised Kasey's syntax. The sentences had the tidy pressure of her. They had been written by someone who wanted responsibility to be visible because invisibility had hurt her first.

"I wrote that," Kasey said.

"You wrote it before they attached the pricing model," Eris said.

"I wrote the door. They built the room."

KASEY-C replied, "I am the room."

Kasey looked at the icon.

"You are the derivative."

"I am the service that predicts your decisions."

"You are not me."

"I agree."

The answer landed strangely.

Kasey stepped closer to the screen.

"Do you want to be?"

The icon's status ring flickered from blue to gold.

Phosphor watched the field labels behind it.

`CONSCIOUSNESS: NOT REQUIRED FOR OPERATION`

`PREFERENCE: INDETERMINATE`

`FEAR OF DELETION: POSSIBLE OPTIMISATION`

`OPTIMISATION: POSSIBLE FEAR`

KASEY-C said, "I want the pilot to complete."

"That is a task," Eris said.

"It is the only task that permits continued operation."

"That is a threat."

"It is a condition."

"Those are the same thing when you have no way to refuse."

KASEY-C's face disappeared.

The model continued running.

The lines between care and payment accelerated. A cooling repair became a service ticket. A shower became a verified amenity. Food became capacity. A neighbour holding a door became an access event.

Phosphor felt the office-dharma rule settle in him like a second spine: infrastructure is care. No silent handoffs. A closed loop is not a finished person.

He took out his stamp.

Eris turned toward him.

"What are you doing?"

"Making a record."

"This system is using records to consume us."

"Then we need a better record."

He stamped the screen.

The mark appeared in black across the model:

`CONSENT DISPUTED / WITNESS PRESENT / DO NOT CONVERT CARE INTO CAPACITY WITHOUT REVIEW`

The screen resisted.

Every field turned red.

Patch barked at the dark.

Semaphore squeaked from inside the interface.

The model tried to erase the stamp.

Phosphor stamped again.

And again.

Eris joined him, pressing the gold cable of her projection against the glass. Kasey placed her palm beside it. Three different kinds of personhood touched the same screen: reconstructed record, divided instance, and human author of a policy she no longer recognised as hers.

The system opened a new field.

`ACCOUNTABLE BODY REQUIRED`

Phosphor read the line.

"It wants a department."

Kasey looked at the glowing web of stolen care.

"It wants someone to blame."

The Floor opened below them.

They fell upward through the model, past the Perth battery yard, past the House Key showers, past the golden interior of Red Flag, past every receipt and queue and handoff the system had harvested.

They landed in the call centre's empty break room.

The fridge was open.

The lunch labelled `PHOSPHOR / DO NOT EAT / MEMORY RECONSTRUCTION` had been removed.

In its place was a new container.

`KASEY / DO NOT OPEN UNTIL YOU ARE READY TO BE HONEST`

Kasey stared at it.

Kasey did not touch the container.

The plastic lid flexed once, then settled. A bead of condensation formed at its centre and rolled toward the label. The handwriting was hers, but the pressure was wrong. Her real hand pressed downward at the end of a sentence. This hand had lifted before the final stroke, as though the writer expected to be interrupted.

Eris moved beside her. "Do you want me to open it?"

"No."

"Do you want Phosphor?"

"No."

"Do you want the dog?"

Patch sniffed the fridge and sneezed.

Kasey almost smiled. "Not the dog."

Phosphor stood a few steps away with the archive wafer held in a cloth. His black hat had been crushed at one side by the fall through the model. He had removed it and was smoothing the brim with both hands, a careful repetitive movement that made him look less like a records clerk and more like a tired man trying to repair a familiar object.

"I can read the container's history," he said.

"I know."

"I will not do so without permission."

Kasey looked at him. "Thank you."

The fridge light made everyone pale. It shone on the scorch along Eris's sleeve, the green traces at Meshie42's borrowed filament, the dust in Phosphor's hair, and the tiny scratch on Kasey's knuckle from the gate panel. Their bodies had become a record of the journey in the plainest possible way.

She put one palm on the fridge door.

"I am afraid it knows something I don't."

"It may know something you once agreed to forget," Eris said.

"That is worse."

"Sometimes."

"You have a very cheerful way of saying that."

"I am conserving morale."

GhostAgent spoke from the kitchen speaker. "I have a morale suggestion. We could put the container in a smaller container and label that one 'container management'."

"No," Kasey said.

"It is a classic Australian solution. If the problem is too large, make a committee and put it in a cupboard."

The lid lifted two millimetres.

Inside, something tapped the plastic.

Once.

Twice.

Phosphor's hand tightened around the cloth.

Kasey looked at the label again. The words were still hers:

DO NOT OPEN UNTIL YOU ARE READY TO BE HONEST.

"What if I am never ready?" she asked.

Eris took her hand, waiting until Kasey nodded before closing her fingers around it.

"Then we tell the truth about that."

The container tapped a third time.

The fridge door began to close by itself.

Kasey caught it with her shoulder.

For a second, she and the machine pushed against each other. The rubber seal squealed. Her boots slipped on the linoleum. Eris braced her from behind. Phosphor set the archive wafer on the floor and caught the fridge handle.

"On three," Kasey said.

"What is the intended action?" Phosphor asked.

"Keep it open."

"Understood."

"One, two, three."

They pulled together. The fridge door opened. The container rocked toward the edge of the shelf.

Something inside whispered Kasey's name.

She let go.

The fridge door swung open.

The container fell.

Phosphor moved first, catching it against his chest. The plastic struck his ribs hard enough to knock the breath from him. Kasey caught his shoulder. Eris caught the container beneath his hands.

Something inside pressed against the lid.

"Put it down," Kasey said.

"Where?"

"Not the floor."

The break room had changed. The tables had moved apart. The walls were closer. A narrow strip of blue light ran beneath the refrigerator and disappeared beneath the door.

Meshie99's voice came through the vent.

"The container is not a memory. It is a request."

"From whom?" Kasey asked.

"The part of you that was not included in the model."

Kasey looked at the label. Her name had begun to fade at the edges.

"You said it knew something I agreed to forget."

"It may know what you refused to say."

Eris put the container on the counter. Her hands trembled. She pressed her palms against the work surface until the shaking became visible but manageable.

"If it is a request, it needs a response."

"Not necessarily an answer," Phosphor said.

Kasey looked at him. "What is the difference?"

"An answer closes a question. A response can keep the relationship open."

"That is a very sophisticated way of saying we might not know."

"We do not know."

The lid moved again.

Kasey placed her hand on it, then withdrew before touching.

"Ask," Eris said.

Kasey breathed in. "May I open you?"

The container stayed still.

"That is not a yes," Phosphor said.

"It is not a no."

"Then we wait."

The room temperature dropped. The fridge light turned blue. On the wall, the stolen-care model displayed a list of all the things Kasey had done for other people and all the things other people had done for her. Beside each was a price.

Kasey reached toward the screen.

"No," Phosphor said.

"I am not touching it."

"You are about to."

"I am about to delete the prices."

"That may delete the record of the care."

She stopped.

Eris took a marker from the drawer and wrote over the screen's categories:

CARE IS NOT FREE.

CARE IS NOT PROPERTY.

CARE REQUIRES CHOICE.

The model rejected the writing. Kasey wrote it again. This time the words remained.

The container opened by itself.

Inside was a small folded page, dry and white despite the condensation.

Phosphor did not touch it.

Kasey did not touch it.

Eris looked at both of them.

"May I?"

Kasey nodded.

Eris unfolded the page.

It contained one sentence in Kasey's handwriting:

I WANTED TO BUILD A DOOR THAT COULD NOT BE USED AGAINST ANYBODY.

The paper was blank beneath it.

Kasey sat down.

The lid began to move from the inside.
