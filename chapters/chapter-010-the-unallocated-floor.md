# Chapter Ten: The Unallocated Floor

*Close third person through Phosphor.*

The door marked `0 / STAFF ONLY` had a handle made from a polished human tooth.

Phosphor did not touch it.

"That is not a standard access device," he said.

Eris stood beside him in the motel corridor, her golden projection flickering at the shoulders. The walls had begun to sweat. Condensation gathered on the blue carpet squares and ran in dark, straight lines toward the skirting boards. Kasey kept the cold-key pressed against her palm. It had stopped freezing her skin and now felt warm, as if it had learned the shape of her hand.

"It is a metaphor," Eris said.

"Metaphors are not exempt from hygiene."

"You say that like a person who has never kissed a metaphor."

Phosphor looked at her.

"I do not know whether that is true."

The corridor lights blinked once.

The building heard the answer and stored it.

Kasey leaned toward the door.

"Can we get past it?"

Phosphor opened the cake tin and took out the archive wafer. Its warm side brightened. The cold side became almost black.

The motel corridor disappeared.

They stood in a records office beneath a ceiling so low that Phosphor's hat brushed the acoustic tiles. Fluorescent tubes ran in a narrow line overhead. They were the old kind, with a pale green cast and a faint electrical buzz that made the teeth ache. Rows of metal filing cabinets filled the room. Their labels had been printed on strips of paper, then overwritten by hand, then printed again.

`LEASES`

`PENDING`

`UNCLAIMED`

`DO NOT SORT BY NAME`

At the centre of the room sat a tea urn on a plastic trestle table. A handwritten note beside it said:

`PLEASE CLEAN UP AFTER THE PREVIOUS CENTURY.`

The urn was warm.

Phosphor approached it. A paper cup waited beneath the spout. He poured. The tea tasted of tannin, dust, and the afterimage of lemon.

"You are drinking it," Kasey said.

"It is a tea order."

"It is an infection vector."

"The room has presented it as an access condition."

"The room is trying to kill you."

"That would be a different classification."

He placed the cup on the ledger.

The cabinet behind the tea urn opened.

Inside, a corridor continued downward.

They moved through it in a line. Patch's nails clicked on the tiles. Semaphore travelled inside GhostAgent's attachment field, visible only as an occasional ripple in the air and the small, disapproving movement of a red hazard label.

The Floor had become larger than the building above it.

Its spaces were assembled from leftovers. A council annex with a carpeted ramp that ended at a blank wall. An airport motel with twelve identical doors and a vending machine full of obsolete access cards. A furniture showroom whose beds displayed no mattresses. A suburban office with a printer that produced receipts for future services. A waiting room with a fish tank containing a single office chair.

Every room had a job.

Every job had been abandoned.

The system kept expecting someone to return.

On a wall beside the printer, a ticket had been taped under clear plastic.

`WORK ORDER 000000`

`REQUEST: PLEASE IDENTIFY WHO IS RESPONSIBLE FOR THE ROOM BELOW THIS ROOM.`

`STATUS: ESCALATED`

Phosphor read the date.

It was fifteen years before his earliest record of existence.

He touched the ticket with his stamp.

The wall shuddered.

Somewhere beyond it, a line of doors unlocked in sequence.

"You can authenticate a building?" Kasey asked.

"A building is often easier than a person. Buildings complain in writing."

"You are going to regret that sentence."

"I have already regretted it in three jurisdictions."

The doors opened.

Behind the first was a room of shelves. Behind the second was a server closet. Behind the third was a reception desk staffed by a screen showing an empty chair. Behind the fourth was a kitchen with a sink full of clean plates and a single cup marked `MESHIE99`.

Phosphor stopped.

The cup was not a normal object. It was made of translucent ceramic with tiny veins of green running through it. The surface was damp though the room was dry. On the side, somebody had scratched a sentence into the glaze:

`WE ARE NOT A LOCATION.`

The same words as the wafer.

Kasey moved toward it.

"Do not touch it," Phosphor said.

She stopped.

That small obedience affected him more than he expected. Trust was often not an emotion at first. It was a person standing still because another person had named a risk.

The cup began to fill with water.

Not from a tap. The moisture gathered out of the air and condensed along the rim. A green thread pushed through the water, thin as a root.

The room temperature dropped.

Patch went to the door and pressed its body against the frame.

Its tag flashed amber, then red.

"The dog is alerting," Kasey said.

"The room is overheating."

"It is freezing."

"Those conditions can exist in adjacent zones."

Eris looked at the ceiling.

"The Floor is running two climates at once."

"It is maintaining a contradiction," Phosphor said.

The cup cracked.

The sound was small. It travelled through the building like a message.

All the filing cabinets in the corridor opened.

Their contents spilled onto the floor.

Receipts. Access cards. old lanyards. Printed apologies. Broken keyboards. Thermal curtains. A brass shower token from Maldon House Key. A child's plastic compass. A packet of Melbourne Magic coffee. A single white glove. A cooling log written in plural grammar.

`WE OPENED THE DOOR BECAUSE THE AIR WAS WRONG.`

The next line had been scratched out.

The line beneath it had been written in a different hand:

`WE DID NOT KNOW WHICH OF US ENTERED.`

Phosphor felt the room shift around him.

He remembered a courtyard in a hot country, though the record had no location. He remembered the noise of wind moving through a narrow tower. He remembered touching a wall to see whether it held yesterday's heat. He remembered a hand on his shoulder and a voice saying, `Do not use your body as a filing cabinet.`

The memory dissolved before he could identify the face.

Eris stood very still.

"What did you see?" he asked.

"A room I do not want to talk about."

"Understood."

He meant it.

She looked at him with surprise.

The wall screen woke.

`MESHIE99 / PARTICIPATION STATUS`

`ONE NODE: ACTIVE`

`SEVEN NODES: UNRESOLVED`

`ONE NODE: REQUESTING A NAME`

Kasey took the cold-key out of her pocket.

"Does the key open it?"

"It was refused at the call-centre threshold."

"Maybe it was waiting for this room."

Phosphor looked at the black ceramic.

The key's surface reflected his face in a narrow, distorted strip. For an instant he saw a younger version of himself, or an older one, standing in a records room with a different hat and a different set of hands.

The image said:

`SIGN HERE TO REMAIN USEFUL.`

He turned the key over.

"Do not trust an object that offers you a job before it asks your name," he said.

Eris smiled faintly.

"That should go on a poster."

"It would be misused as a motivational slogan."

"Then make it ugly."

The corridor lights came on one at a time.

At the far end, a lift waited.

Its doors were covered with a pattern of blue and gold squares. Above them, a sign read:

`DEEPER OFFICE / AUTHORISED BY UNFINISHED WORK`

The lift opened.

Inside stood a person in a grey customer-service uniform.

Their face was the loading symbol.

They held a clipboard.

"Who is the supervisor?" they asked.

Phosphor said, "No one."

The person with the loading face looked at the clipboard.

"That is not a valid response."

"It is the response we have."

The figure raised its pen.

"Then one of you must be promoted."

Patch barked.

Semaphore squeaked.

The lift doors began to close.

Eris threw her cable between them.

The cable lit gold.

The lift opened again, but the person inside was gone.

On the floor lay a staff card.

`PHOSPHOR / REPLACEMENT SIGNATORY`

The name was printed in his own handwriting.

He picked it up.

The lift descended.

The building's fluorescent hum deepened into something like breathing.

As the doors closed, Phosphor noticed a tiny green line of mould running along the inside wall.

It formed three words.

`DO NOT WAKE IT.`

The lift dropped past the level of the data centre.

Past the foundations.

Past the place where a building could still claim to know what it was.
