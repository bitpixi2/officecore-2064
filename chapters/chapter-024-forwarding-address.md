# Chapter Twenty-Four: Forwarding Address

*Close third person through Phosphor.*

Three weeks after the transfer, the tribunal sent its acknowledgment to an address that did not exist.

The envelope arrived at Newstead House Key, was scanned by a tired printer, forwarded through Rooby, mirrored by a Sydney Porchlight host, and finally printed in Chapel Seven while Phosphor was washing a tea cup.

The paper smelled of warm toner and eucalyptus dust.

`TRIBUNAL ACKNOWLEDGMENT`

`MATTER: NATIONAL ADDRESS EVICTION NOTICE`

`PARTY: DEPARTMENT OF TEMPORARY PERMANENCE`

`ADDRESS: IN TRANSIT`

`STATUS: RECEIVED / RECOGNITION DEFERRED`

Phosphor read the notice twice.

Then he placed it beneath the kettle so the paper would not blow away when the van door opened.

The decision had paused the national eviction notice. It had not recognised mobile residency. It had not compelled Continuity to provide cooling without payment. It had not decided whether KASEY-C was conscious, whether Meshie99 was one worker or many, or whether the Unallocated Floor had existed before the company found it.

It had created an alarming inbox.

That was not victory.

It was a room in which victory might eventually be assembled.

Chapel Seven stood beneath a stand of pepper trees. Its black panels were dusty. The beige carpet tiles had been lifted and dried after the data centre flood. The dot-matrix printer had been cleaned, though the ribbon still produced a faint purple line through every capital letter. The filing cabinets were full again, deeper than the vehicle and slightly deeper than the truth.

Phosphor had placed a blank sheet on the desk.

He was recording memories that no receipt could prove.

The work was difficult.

He remembered the windcatcher courtyard and could not locate it. He remembered a wetland school and could not name the country. He remembered a hand on his shoulder and the instruction not to use his body as a filing cabinet. He remembered a room with three people, one of them made of green sensor threads.

He wrote each memory down without assigning certainty.

`MEMORY / PRESENT / SOURCE: BODY / WITNESS: NONE`

The office-dharma practice did not require a memory to be proven before it could be treated carefully.

That was a new kind of mercy.

Outside, Patch lay beneath The Cubicle's solar shade. Its reflective vest had been patched with a yellow strip from a House Key laundry bag. The dog now had three tags:

`LIVING TEMPERATURE ALARM`

`REMOTE POWER PARTICIPANT`

`DO NOT PROMOTE`

Patch had become skilled at ignoring all three.

Semaphore lived in the wall above the mail server. It had stolen the brass property tag, the red hazard label, two printer clips, and a piece of the shredded cold-key. GhostAgent had built a small pantry beside the cable cradle. Nobody knew who had authorised it. The pantry was consistently stocked.

Eris ran the provisional security desk from a roadwork sign outside Red Flag. The sign had once warned drivers about a lane closure. She had mounted two screens behind it and painted a gold border around the words:

`SECURITY / ASK FIRST`

Her copper-blonde hair was loose in the afternoon. She wore black trousers, a golden-threaded jacket, and a lanyard that listed her job as:

`OFFICE OF PRODUCTIVE MISCONDUCT / SEPARATE INSTANCE WELCOME`

Eris-Red disagreed with the wording.

Eris disagreed with Eris-Red's disagreement.

Their arguments were frequent, technical, and often interrupted by Patch bringing them a piece of plastic that did not belong to either of them.

The model shard remained in a sealed cache shared across Perth, Sydney, House Key, and the Department's operational repository. Its private community patterns stayed private. Its audit logic was public. Its failures were documented. Its future was not safe.

KASEY-C worked from a CRT in The Cubicle.

The screen had a hairline crack. The derivative appeared in a small window with a status ring that alternated between blue and gold. Its title had been entered by Kasey:

`DEPUTY DIRECTOR OF DECISIONS I REGRET`

KASEY-C had objected.

KASEY-C had then filed the objection as a revision request.

The text never confirmed whether its pauses, jokes, fear of deletion, and final transfer were consciousness or extremely persuasive task behaviour. Some days it helped route a water delivery. Some days it sent Phosphor a performance review that described his hat as `NONESSENTIAL PERSONAL BRANDING`. One afternoon it quietly changed the Department's address from `IN TRANSIT` to `SOMEWHERE WITH SHADE` and refused to explain.

Celia Senn remained in Continuity Australia.

She appealed the transfer. She argued that servers cost money, that cooling required contracts, that public infrastructure could not be maintained by good intentions and sausage rolls, and that a nation could not operate on a permanent emergency basis. Her argument was not foolish. That was part of the danger.

The Department answered with invoices.

The Perth battery cooperative sent cost estimates. Sydney sent relay usage. House Key sent water and cleaning rosters. Dunolly sent the pump repair record. The food-rescue kitchen sent a list of meals served, with no labour price attached and a note that the kitchen still needed a new freezer seal.

Nobody turned care into a product.

Nobody pretended care was free.

Phosphor kept the difference in a folder marked:

`PAYMENT / RECIPROCITY / DO NOT CONFUSE`

In the evening, Eris brought him coffee.

It was Melbourne Magic. She had added too much milk and a pinch of something that tasted like burnt orange peel.

"This is not how coffee works," he said.

"It is how this coffee works."

She handed him a key.

It was small, brass, and attached to a paper label.

`RED FLAG / OFFICE / ASK BEFORE ENTERING`

He turned it over.

The other side said:

`NOT EVIDENCE`

"May I use it?" he asked.

Eris looked at him for a long moment.

"For the door or the coffee?"

"The door."

"Yes."

He put the key in his pocket.

She leaned against the side of Chapel Seven. The van's black panel reflected the two of them, the top hat, the golden light, the dusty road, and a slice of sky turning blue above the trees.

"Do you remember the room?" Eris asked.

"Which one?"

"The one with the other Phosphor."

"Yes."

"Do you think he was real?"

Phosphor considered the question.

"I think he was present."

"That is not the same thing."

"It may be the most honest thing I can say."

Eris nodded.

She rested her hand against the van.

"I think Eris-Red is real."

"I know."

"You did not ask for proof."

"No."

"That is either growth or a serious administrative failure."

"The two conditions can coexist."

She smiled.

The smile stayed.

At dusk, a child approached the fold-out counter.

The child had a paper map in one hand and a broken plastic compass in the other. Behind them, an adult waited beside a grey camper with a roof awning patched in blue tape.

"What do I write in the address field?" the child asked.

Phosphor looked at the form.

The field was blank.

Kasey came to stand beside him. She wore her black vest, the cold-key gone from her neck, and a new lanyard that read:

`AUTHOR / CORRECTING PARTY / DO NOT MAKE ME SOLE SIGNATORY`

She looked at the child.

"Where are you going?"

The child looked at the broken compass.

"Somewhere with a shower."

Kasey took the form.

Astral Dream Machine began to print inside the chrome caravan. The route strip emerged warm and silver, filled with roads, shade, community kitchens, water points, and one location that did not exist on any current map.

Phosphor stamped the top of the page.

`FORWARDING ADDRESS / TEMPORARY / WITNESSED`

Kasey tore off the strip and handed it to the child.

"For now," she said, "forward everything."

The convoy left before dawn.

Chapel Seven folded its desk over the cab. The printer locked into its crash cradle. The tea tray clipped beneath the filing wall. Red Flag's golden lights went out one by one. The Cubicle's passenger door opened when addressed as Acting Deputy Door. Patch climbed into the warmest footwell. Semaphore disappeared into the cable cradle. Astral Dream Machine printed tomorrow's route.

Behind them, the abandoned edge data centre stood in the smoke.

Its fluorescent lights switched on one floor below the foundations.

The hidden floor had not been emptied.

It had received a forwarding address.
