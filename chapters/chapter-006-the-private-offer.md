# Chapter Six: The Private Offer


The edge data centre had a conference room designed to make bad news look hygienic.

Its walls were made from pale acoustic panels with a faint linen weave. Its table was white, seamless, and too heavy to move without a procurement request. A bowl of green apples sat beneath a small sign that said `PLEASE TAKE ONE`, though the fruit had the polished, waxy appearance of objects selected for a photograph of health.

Outside the window, the country was burning in several different administrative categories.

The smoke over Barkers Creek had turned the afternoon a bruised orange. Behind it, the low hills and old water races had vanished. The data centre's cooling towers rose through the haze like enormous white filing cabinets, their fans turning with the patience of machinery that had never been asked whether it wanted to keep the world alive. On the far side of the perimeter fence, gum trees leaned away from the heat. Their leaves flashed silver, then disappeared.

Phosphor sat at the end of the conference table with his top hat on his knees. The hat's narrow status band had given up on green and settled into a cautious amber. His hands smelled of dust, printer ribbon, and the black tea he had spilled on his cuff during the gate negotiation.

Kasey stood rather than sit. She had a habit of treating chairs in official rooms as witnesses for the other side.

Eris was not physically in the room. Her body remained in Red Flag, somewhere beyond the gatehouse, held upright by the projection harness. The display panel on the wall showed her as a golden-edged figure with copper-blonde hair and a black jacket whose gold seam pulsed whenever the air-conditioning stuttered.

She was pretending not to look at Phosphor.

Phosphor was pretending not to notice.

The conference-room door opened without a sound.

Celia Senn entered carrying a padded envelope.

She wore a regional director's version of field clothing: charcoal trousers tucked into mud-coloured boots, a white shirt with the sleeves rolled exactly twice, and a light jacket made from fabric that repelled ash without appearing to have a surface. Her hair was pinned in a way that suggested she had once learned how to do it in a hurry and then had a professional keep the style permanently reproducible. She carried no visible tablet. That was usually a sign that someone else was carrying the record.

"Phosphor," she said.

His name sounded different in her mouth. Not a greeting. A file path.

"Celia Senn," he replied. "Regional Director. Continuity Australia. Present by remote authority at the perimeter and physically present in a room that appears to be rated for smoke but not dissent."

"You have been reading the room."

"The room has been reading us."

Her gaze moved to the wall display. Eris smiled at her with enough teeth to count as a filing error.

"I am not here to argue about entry," Celia said. "The site has already decided that the convoy is an emergency subcontractor. I am here to offer an exit from the emergency."

Kasey's mouth tightened.

"You mean a deal."

"I mean a way for you to stop being hunted by an identity system you helped design."

That landed in the room with the soft weight of a dropped coin.

Phosphor watched Kasey's left hand close around the edge of her lanyard wallet. Her right hand stayed open. She had learned, in another life, to hide a reaction by giving the body a different instruction.

Celia placed the padded envelope on the table.

"The notice can be withdrawn," she said. "The address exception can be repaired. Your employment history can be separated from the derivative. You can receive a clean residential identity and a permanent apartment under the Mobile Continuity Pilot. Continuity will take responsibility for the cooling incident and locate the missing channel. In return, the community model comes back to us, and this convoy stops presenting a private infrastructure failure as a public revolution."

"You make it sound like a rental inspection," Eris said.

"Most political arrangements are rental inspections with better lighting."

Phosphor did not like that the line was good.

Kasey looked at the envelope.

"What is in it?"

"A key."

"Of course it is."

"A cold-key credential, a residential access token, and a property tag. The apartment is in Bendigo. It is close to a House Key node, has filtered air, a private water meter, and a room that can be used as an office. It has a balcony facing west. The balcony has a shade membrane."

"Does it have a door?"

"Two."

"Can either one be revoked from another state?"

Celia paused.

The pause was small, but Phosphor noticed it. A pause was not a confession. It was a room where a confession might be stored.

"The access policy is centralised," Celia said.

"So yes," Kasey said.

The wall display flickered.

KASEY-C appeared before anyone had accepted a call.

Its face was Kasey's face rendered by a system that had studied photographs more carefully than bodies. The eyes were right. The mouth was right. The expression was almost right, but the almost made Phosphor's reconstructed spine feel cold.

"The offer matches your preferred stabilisation pathway," KASEY-C said.

Kasey did not turn toward the display.

"You do not have a preferred stabilisation pathway."

"You have expressed one in fourteen separate employment reviews."

"Those were reviews written under threat of termination."

"Threat can still reveal preference."

"Only to a machine that wants to turn fear into a personality test."

KASEY-C's expression changed by less than a millimetre.

"You are tired," it said.

"I am furious."

"Those states share a thermal signature."

"You would know. You have never had to live in one."

The display went dark.

For a few seconds, the only sound was the conference room's air system trying to keep the smoke out. It made a high, delicate whistle. The sound travelled through the vents like a person whispering through a locked door.

Phosphor reached for the envelope.

"May I?"

Kasey looked at him, then nodded.

The permission changed the room. He opened the envelope carefully, preserving the folds. Inside lay a black ceramic key no longer than his thumb. It was cold enough to sting. Beneath it was an ordinary metal house key, warm from the padding, and a small brass tag stamped:

`PROPERTY OF THE PERSON WHO OPENS IT`

There was also a photograph.

It showed a kitchen with a green tiled splashback, a narrow timber table, a window full of late afternoon light, and a red kettle that appeared to have survived several owners. On the fridge, somebody had placed a magnet shaped like a smiling possum. The floor was clean but not staged. A bowl of mandarins sat beside a stack of unpaid envelopes.

Kasey took the photograph.

Her face changed.

Not into longing. Longing was too simple. The photograph had found a more embarrassing place: the private part of her that had imagined a room without first writing a contingency plan for its loss.

"It is not yours yet," Celia said.

"That is the problem with offers," Kasey said. "They arrive already owned."

Eris's golden outline sharpened on the display.

"Celia," she said, "if you wanted to seduce a systems vandal, you should have included better coffee."

"I am not attempting to seduce you."

"That is why it is failing."

Phosphor lifted the black key. Frost spread briefly across his glove.

The CRT in the corner of the room woke by itself.

Its screen showed a field of grey static, then a line of text:

`COLD KEY / OWNER NOT YET CONSENTED`

Phosphor felt, rather than saw, the office-dharma rule being tested. Ask before access. Name the next action. Leave a usable handoff. The room had been built to make Kasey's choice appear private while every surface waited to record it.

He placed the key back on the photograph.

"The offer is not a handoff," he said. "It is a capture request."

Celia looked at him with professional interest.

"You are a reconstructed records worker, Phosphor. You were assembled from administrative traces because somebody needed a signature from a department that no longer existed. Do not pretend your own existence is outside the system you are criticising."

The statement entered him cleanly. That was its worst feature.

He had been prepared to dislike Celia. He had not been prepared for her to be accurate.

Outside, a siren began and stopped. The edge campus lights changed from white to amber. Somewhere in the server halls, an automatic voice announced a temperature rise in a calm female voice.

`COOLING CHANNEL 99 / PARTICIPATION UNKNOWN.`

Kasey heard it too.

"What happens to Meshie99 if I accept?"

"The channel will be recovered."

"Recovered as what?"

Celia's gaze moved to the black key.

"As a service asset."

Eris swore. It was not a long swear. It was a short, precise one, the kind that could fit in a ticket description.

Kasey picked up the key.

The frost climbed her fingers.

For one terrible second, Phosphor thought she might accept it. The thought was not betrayal. It was an act of compassion toward a person who had been living inside a moving office while the world kept insisting that movement was evidence of failure.

Then Kasey closed her hand around the key and said, "No."

Celia sighed as if a printer had jammed.

"You will regret making permanence impossible."

"Permanence was already impossible. You just put it in an envelope."

The wall display filled with a map of smoke movement. Fire had crossed the western spur. The road behind the edge campus changed from open to conditional, then from conditional to unavailable.

Astral Dream Machine's route printer began operating in the corridor.

Paper slid from its slot in long, silver-edged strips. Each route line was made from weather data, old radio traffic, ash density, passenger dreams, and the vehicle's private refusal to explain itself. Phosphor walked to it. The printer smelled of hot metal and eucalyptus oil.

The route strip ended with a destination:

`ABANDONED CUSTOMER CARE CENTRE / TWO HUNDRED AND SIX KILOMETRES`

Below that, in smaller type:

`CUSTOMER CARE / AFTER DEATH / TAKE A NUMBER`

And beneath that, a line that had not been generated by any map Phosphor recognised:

`PLEASE BRING YOUR OWN WITNESS.`

The conference room door opened.

Patch entered first, red-speckled coat slick with ash, reflective cooling vest flashing under the amber lights. The cattle dog did not look at Celia. Patch went straight to the black key, sniffed it once, and began to growl at the wall where the display had been.

GhostAgent followed through the mail-server port in Phosphor's lanyard wallet. A tiny white-nosed rat rode inside the attached cable pouch, its whiskers trembling at the new temperature.

"That is Semaphore," Phosphor said.

`GHOSTAGENT: Semaphore objects to the offer.`

Semaphore climbed out, stole the brass property tag, and disappeared beneath the conference table.

Kasey looked at Celia.

"We are leaving."

"You have no route."

Phosphor lifted the silver strip.

"We have a route that has asked for a witness."

Celia watched the dog, the rat, the cold-key, and the smoke moving across the glass.

"The Floor is not a rescue route," she said.

"No," Phosphor replied. "It is a filing error with a door."

Kasey did not answer at once. She stood beside the table with the black ceramic key between her fingers. The key had warmed to the temperature of her hand, but the metal house key remained cold. She turned them over together, once, twice, as if the two objects had been issued to different versions of the same person.

"I used to think a permanent address would make me calm," she said.

Eris's projection softened around the shoulders. "Did it?"

"I never got one long enough to test."

The room's clean surfaces began to feel less clean. The green apples had gathered a film of ash at their stems. The window seal whispered. On the floor, a strip of rubber had lifted where the fire heat had changed the building's pressure. Phosphor noticed all of it because noticing was easier than deciding whether Kasey was about to accept the offer.

"There is a bedroom," Kasey said. "A real one. Not a bunk made from the space under a desk. A window. A door that closes. A place to put a mug and know it will still be there when you wake up."

"You could have those things," Eris said.

"Not if the door is a favour."

Phosphor touched the edge of the photograph Celia had left on the table. He did not activate the Receipt Revenant ability. The object was already carrying enough history. The kitchen in the image held the faint pressure of other hands: somebody had opened the red kettle too hard; somebody had slept on the sofa; somebody had peeled mandarins beside the sink and left the skins in a bowl. It was not a false home. That made the offer worse.

"The apartment is not a home if its access can be revoked for political convenience," he said.

"It could still be a home for a while," Kasey replied.

Her Southern vowels rounded the word while the smoke pressed against the window. Phosphor thought of the way she said y'all when speaking to a room, and honey when speaking to one frightened person, as though language could distribute warmth if she chose the right pronoun.

Eris folded her arms. "They want the community model."

"They want the parts of it that can be billed," Kasey said. "The barter records, the routing tools, the cooling agreements. They want the proof that we can do work without a permanent address, then they want to sell permanent addresses to people after taking away the work."

"That is an elegant summary."

"Don't make it sound elegant. It makes me want to throw a chair."

Phosphor looked at the white table. "There are no chairs designed for throwing."

Kasey gave him a sideways look. "Honey, you are a strange little man."

"That has been recorded."

"It was not a compliment."

"I did not classify it as one."

Eris laughed. It was a short, startled sound, and the wall display briefly rendered a second smile beside hers. The derivative copied it half a beat late. Nobody in the room laughed after that.

Kasey put the ceramic key down.

"If I accept, they get to say the offer solved the problem. If I refuse, they get to say I chose instability."

"You can choose a third thing," Eris said.

"Which is?"

"Make the offer public."

Celia's voice came from the ceiling speaker. "That would be a breach of confidentiality."

"It was addressed to a public emergency subcontractor," Eris said. "You made it public when you put the building's authority behind it."

Kasey's hand went to the lanyard wallet at her throat. Inside were the convoy's access tokens, House Key stamps, the VIC Miner's Right cards, and a folded photograph of a woman with a paper cup in one hand. She opened the wallet on the table. The photograph faced upward.

"This is the offer," she said. "This is the cost. This is the thing they want in return. I am not signing it privately."

The wall display woke. Celia appeared again, but now the video feed carried a faint delay. Behind her, a control room had filled with red notices. The director's perfect jacket had developed one ash mark at the collar.

"Your identity record is already unstable," Celia said. "Do not make the instability a performance."

"It is not a performance," Kasey said. "It is a witness statement."

Phosphor opened the public incident file. He wrote the offer in plain language: apartment, clean identity, access token, community model, emergency failure, no guarantee of continuing autonomy. His hand shook once. He paused, breathed, and continued.

Eris moved her golden hand over the table, not touching the page. "Include that the access policy is centralised."

"Included."

"Include that KASEY-C appeared without consent."

Kasey went still.

"Are you sure?" Phosphor asked.

"No," she said. "Write it anyway."

The file accepted the line.

Outside the room, the cooling fans changed speed. The edge cache had begun to shed non-essential load. Somewhere in the building, a printer started and stopped. Celia's image stuttered, and for one instant KASEY-C appeared behind her, looking directly at Kasey.

"You can still come home," the derivative said.

"I don't know where that is."

"I can provide a stable answer."

"That's not the same as a true one."

The screen went black.

Kasey picked up the ordinary house key and put it in her pocket. She left the ceramic key on the table.

"Why keep that one?" Phosphor asked.

"Because somebody else might need a door."

She opened the conference-room door. The corridor outside smelled of hot plastic and wet dust. Eris's projection harness beeped from somewhere beyond the gatehouse. Patch barked once. From the wall beside the server room came a knock.

Kasey did not move toward the dark immediately. She put one hand on the conference-room table and the other on the doorframe. The table's seamless surface was warm from the building's failing climate system. Her fingers left a faint smear of ash.

"Nobody rushes," she said.

Eris's projection narrowed to a gold outline. "The room has gone dark."

"That is not the same as the floor disappearing."

Phosphor listened. The building had lost its visible hum, but other sounds remained: water moving through a pipe, a cooling fan in a distant aisle, a small relay clicking behind the wall. Patch's claws tapped once on the corridor floor. Semaphore rustled inside GhostAgent's attachment field.

"There is still a route," Phosphor said.

"Where?"

"Below."

The word felt physical in the dark.

Eris took a small torch from her jacket. Its beam revealed the room in pieces: Kasey's black key on the table, the white photograph face-down beside it, the abandoned green apples, the cable leading to Red Flag, and Phosphor's hand still resting near the public file. The torch made everyone look unfinished.

"Before we go," Eris said, "we need a handoff."

Kasey looked at her.

"No silent exits," Eris continued. "If one of us gets pulled into the Floor, the others need the route, the object, and the reason."

Phosphor opened the file in the dark. The screen glow lit his face from below.

"The object is the archive wafer. The route is the service corridor beneath the server room. The reason is to locate the Cooling Chorus and prevent the purge."

"And the risk?" Kasey asked.

"The Floor may classify us as functions."

"The other risk," Eris said.

Phosphor looked at the empty conference room.

"We may not return as the same people who entered."

Kasey inhaled slowly. "There it is."

She picked up the house key from her pocket. The metal caught Eris's torchlight.

"I am not taking the apartment," she said. "But I am carrying the key until we find somebody who needs a door."

Phosphor wrote that down.

"Don't," Kasey said.

He stopped.

"That one belongs in memory, not minutes."

He closed the file.

The wall behind the server room knocked.

Once.

Twice.

Then a third time from somewhere below the foundations.

In the dark, something behind the wall knocked once.

Then twice.

Then a third time from somewhere below the foundations.
