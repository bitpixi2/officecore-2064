# Chapter Fourteen: Phosphor's Audit


Phosphor began the audit at 3:17 in the morning because the rigs had finally gone quiet and because a person who had been reconstructed from records did not trust daylight to tell the truth.

The temporary department had settled in an industrial estate outside Ballarat. The place had once manufactured office furniture. Its warehouse walls were corrugated iron painted a blue that had faded toward grey. Rain had not fallen in weeks, but the gutters still held the dark smell of old water. Across the road, a sign for a closed carpet outlet leaned against a fence.

Chapel Seven was parked beneath a shade sail borrowed from a House Key node. The sail snapped in the hot wind. Solar panels covered the roof like black leaves. A thermal curtain separated the cab from the office, holding a cool pocket around the desk while the battery fed the printer, the CRT, and the mail server.

Phosphor had moved the bunk down only halfway. He preferred to sleep beneath the files rather than inside them.

Semaphore slept in the cable cradle above the server fan. Patch lay on the gravel with its nose pointed toward the warehouse. Eris was in Golden Apple, where the golden office light showed through the side curtains in a narrow band. Kasey had taken The Cubicle's passenger seat and was pretending to rest while reading the failed vote's comments.

The audit began with a coffee stain.

Phosphor found it on a carbon copy of Kasey's old policy draft. The paper had been folded into a maintenance folder, then carried through at least four offices, a flood, and one period of compulsory digital archiving. The stain covered the middle of a sentence but left the verbs visible.

`CONTINUITY REQUIRES FIXED...`

He laid the page flat beneath the desk lamp.

The carbon copy smelled of paper dust and bitter coffee. Its fibres had softened at the folds. Someone had underlined a clause in blue pencil, then crossed the underline out with red ink.

`MOBILE CAPACITY MUST BE RESOLVED INTO REGISTERED SERVICE UNITS.`

The language was Kasey's. Phosphor knew this not because he had seen her handwriting, but because of the order in which the sentence refused to be kind.

Kasey entered the office carrying two mugs.

"One is Magic," she said. "One is pretending to be Magic because the packet was almost empty."

She wore a pale work shirt beneath a black vest, her sleeves rolled to the exact point where the fabric stopped catching on cable drawers. The cold-key hung on a chain around her neck now. It rested against her collarbone, a black sliver beneath the throat.

Phosphor took the pretending coffee.

It tasted of roasted grain, hot water, and a faint metallic note from the induction plate.

"This is not coffee," he said.

"It is a warning."

"I appreciate the distinction."

She sat opposite him.

"How bad is it?"

"The policy?"

"Me."

Phosphor looked down at the documents.

He had been trained, or rebuilt, to answer questions by finding the correct record. He knew what a legal identity was supposed to sound like. He knew how to distinguish a correction from a deletion, an amendment from a forgery, a missing signature from an unsigned page.

He did not know how to answer a person asking whether they were the bad part of a machine.

"You wrote a framework," he said. "Continuity used it to build an extraction system. Those are both true."

Kasey nodded.

"That is the answer I expected."

"It is not a punishment."

"It feels like one."

"Punishment is not the same as consequence."

"You are very good at making pain sound administratively useful."

He looked at her.

"I am trying to keep it from becoming meaningless."

Outside, Patch barked at a truck that had not stopped.

Semaphore stirred in the cable cradle.

The server fan made a small cough.

Phosphor opened a second folder.

It contained an employment record from Continuity's reconstruction programme. The document was printed on grey paper with a security watermark. The title was:

`ARCHIVAL CONTINUITY SPECIALIST / DECEASED DEPARTMENT RESTORATION`

The applicant name had been blacked out.

The signature had not.

Phosphor recognised it.

His own hand.

The job description listed duties:

- restore an inactive records chain
- authenticate a prior signatory
- maintain historical continuity during a department closure
- do not disclose the reconstruction process to the reconstructed party

He read the last line twice.

Kasey saw his face change.

"What?"

He slid the document across the desk.

She read it without touching the page.

"They built you for a signature."

"Apparently."

"Do you know whose?"

"The record does not say."

"That is convenient."

"Convenience is not evidence."

"You sound like you are defending them."

The sentence entered him like a blade placed carefully between ribs.

He wanted to say that he was not defending anyone. He wanted to say that the exact shape of a truth mattered even when a less exact truth felt better in the mouth.

Instead, he reached for the stamp.

The blank sheet on his desk waited.

`THE AUDIT CANNOT BE COMPLETE UNTIL THE AUDITOR IS INCLUDED.`

He wrote it.

Then he added:

`PHOSPHOR / ORIGIN UNCONFIRMED / CURRENT WITNESS PRESENT`

Kasey read the line.

"You cannot audit yourself and call it independent."

"Correct."

"Then why do it?"

"Because the alternative is allowing the company to audit me alone."

Her fingers moved toward the page, then stopped before contact.

"May I?" she asked.

He nodded.

Kasey touched the paper.

The stamp mark changed colour.

The black ink separated into fine blue threads. A hidden paragraph appeared beneath the line.

`EARLY PILOT SUBJECT WAS NOT CHOSEN FOR MEMORY QUALITY.`

`SUBJECT WAS CHOSEN FOR COMPLIANCE WITH HANDOFF PROCEDURE.`

`SUBJECT RECONSTRUCTED AFTER DEPARTMENTAL DEATH.`

Phosphor felt the temperature in the van drop.

"Departmental death," he said.

Kasey looked toward the filing wall.

"That is what they call a closure when it has no body."

The CRT blinked on.

It displayed a list of departments.

`OFFICE OF REGIONAL MEMORY`

`BUREAU OF TEMPORARY ADDRESSING`

`DEPARTMENT OF INDOOR WEATHER`

`MOBILE CONTINUITY PILOT`

Beside each name was a status.

`CLOSED`

`PENDING`

`ABSORBED`

`ACTIVE / UNALLOCATED`

The last status belonged to `PHOSPHOR / RECORDS FUNCTION`.

He stared at it.

The printer began to run.

Paper emerged in short, nervous bursts. The first sheet showed a lunch order: peaches, bread, a small packet of salt. The second showed a bloodless death certificate with no body attached. The third showed a service ticket that pings every Tuesday at 9:00 a.m.

`REVIEW SIGNATORY AVAILABLE?`

The fourth sheet was a photograph.

It showed a room with high windows and a long table. Three people sat around it. One wore a black top hat. One had copper-blonde hair. The third was only a blur of green sensor threads.

Phosphor did not remember the photograph.

Eris appeared in the doorway.

"You have a picture of us," she said.

"I do not know when it was taken."

She crossed the narrow office, looked at the paper, and went quiet.

The green threads in the photograph leaned toward her.

"That is not me," she said.

"It resembles you."

Eris put two fingers against the photograph without touching it.

"The green threads are facing the camera."

"They are facing you."

"That is not the same."

Phosphor moved the photograph beneath the lamp. The paper had been printed on cheap stock, but the image contained depth. Dust floated in the depicted room. A cup on the table had a dark ring at its base. The third figure was not blurred by poor focus; it had been blurred by a process that did not know how to describe a body made of more than one person.

Kasey leaned into the doorway. She had changed into a wool jumper despite the heat, because the industrial estate's night wind had turned cold inside the rigs. Her American Southern cadence softened when she was tired.

"What are we looking at?"

"A photograph of a meeting that may not have happened," Phosphor said.

"That is a lot of meetings."

Eris kept looking at the green figure. "What was the office called?"

Phosphor turned the page over.

The back held a handwritten note:

ASK THE ARCHIVIST WHY THE ROOM WAS COOL.

His stomach tightened.

He remembered the first time he had noticed heat as a moral fact. It had been in a stone courtyard where shade moved across the ground more slowly than the people beneath it. He had been young enough to believe an efficient building could solve everything. An older person had shown him a damp cloth hung across a doorway, a roof vent opened with a length of string, and a wall painted pale enough to refuse the sun.

The lesson had not been that technology failed. The lesson had been that technology was always held by hands.

Phosphor opened the audit log and added the photograph as an unverified exhibit. He described the three figures: one reconstructed archivist, one copper-haired systems vandal, one plural sensor-body. He added the room's temperature and the fact that the image had produced no matching timestamp.

Kasey watched him write.

"Do you feel better when the page has more information?"

"Sometimes."

"Do you feel better when the information is bad?"

"No."

"Then why keep adding it?"

He looked at the black ink on his fingers.

"Because somebody will need to know what kind of bad it was."

Kasey sat on the edge of the bunk. The spring beneath her made a tired squeak. She rubbed the cold-key through her shirt.

"That is the most Phosphor answer you have ever given."

"I am not certain whether that is criticism."

"It is affection with a little weather on it."

The phrase stopped him. He did not know where to place affection in the ledger. It had no clear owner, no fixed address, and no expiry date. He wrote nothing.

Eris reached for the pretending coffee on the desk.

"May I?"

Kasey pushed it toward her. Eris drank, grimaced, and handed it to Phosphor.

"You have both ruined coffee," Eris said.

"The coffee was already ruined," Kasey replied.

Outside, the shade sail snapped hard enough to make Patch lift its head. The warehouse sign creaked on its fence. In Chapel Seven, the printer woke without being asked.

It fed out a single page.

Phosphor took it.

The page carried a line in green ink:

THE ROOM WAS COOL BECAUSE SOMEONE STAYED.

He looked toward the dark window.

Eris was no longer in the doorway.

Phosphor found her outside Chapel Seven, sitting on the rear step with her boots on the gravel. The shade sail moved above her. Its fabric snapped, relaxed, and snapped again. The gold light from Golden Apple was reflected in the side panel of the black camper, where it made a second office appear in the metal.

"You left," he said.

"I walked three metres."

"The record says you were no longer in the doorway."

"The record is dramatic."

He sat beside her. The step was narrow. Their shoulders touched when the van shifted on its suspension.

Eris held the green-ink page between both hands.

"I don't remember staying in that room," she said.

"I do not remember being there."

"That is not reassuring."

"No."

She bent the page slightly, then flattened it again.

"What happens if the photograph is real?"

"It becomes evidence."

"What happens if it is false?"

"It becomes evidence of a falsehood."

"You really will put anything in a folder."

"Not anything. Some things belong in a conversation."

She looked at him. Her eyes were tired, bright at the edges.

"Is this a conversation?"

Phosphor considered the question. Around them, the rigs made small domestic sounds: the pump in The Cubicle, the printer fan, Patch turning in its sleep, a cup set down inside Golden Apple.

"Yes."

"Then tell me what you are afraid of."

He placed the audit page on his knee.

"That I am only useful when somebody else has made a mistake."

Eris's expression changed.

"That is not what you are."

"It may be what I was built to be."

"Built and used are not the same as chosen."

"The distinction is not yet proven."

"You are going to have to live with some unproven things."

He looked at the green threads on the page.

"I am trying."

Eris folded the paper once and placed it in his hand.

"Then keep this. Not as evidence. As a reminder that someone stayed."

Phosphor put the page inside his jacket.

The shade sail snapped above them.

For the first time that night, he did not write down the sound.

"That is worse."

Kasey picked up the photo.

The third figure moved.

Its head turned toward the camera.

Its face did not resolve.

Under the image, a caption appeared:

`COOLING RESPONSE / WITNESS NOT YET ASSIGNED`

Meshie99 came through the van radio.

"We remember the room."

Phosphor looked at the speaker.

"Were you there?"

"Some of us."

"Which of you?"

"The question is warm."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the answer we can keep."

The radio clicked off.

Kasey placed the photograph face down.

"The audit is not going to tell us who you were," she said.

Phosphor looked at his stamp, his documents, the faint green status light under the CRT.

"No," he said. "It is going to tell us what they needed me to be."

The van door chimed.

Outside, the industrial estate had vanished behind a wall of smoke.

The temporary department's pending repository received a new commit.

`AUTHOR: PHOSPHOR`

`MESSAGE: I WAS BUILT TO SIGN FOR A DEAD OFFICE.`

Then, below it, in a different hand:

`WHAT IF THE OFFICE WAS NOT DEAD?`
