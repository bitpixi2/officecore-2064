# Chapter Eight: The Road Closure Lottery


The road out of Newstead offered four versions of itself.

On Kasey's dashboard it ran east through a sequence of green pixels, past a bridge marked safe and a fuel station marked open. On Astral Dream Machine's chrome route printer it curved north, then became a dotted line through a place called `OLD OFFICE / NEW WEATHER`. Eris's traffic-screen Afterimage showed a highway full of abandoned shopping trolleys. Phosphor's paper copy showed the road as it had appeared in receipts: tyre pressure, coffee stops, a missing hubcap, and the exact cost of a chicken sandwich in Castlemaine.

Patch refused all four.

The cattle dog stood at the junction with its crooked ear raised, air-quality tag blinking amber, and stared at a narrow culvert beneath the road.

"That is not a road," Kasey said.

Patch looked at her.

"It is a drainage structure."

Patch looked at Phosphor.

"It may be a road in another administrative jurisdiction," Phosphor said.

Patch sat down.

The convoy waited while smoke passed over the paddocks. The sky had gone the colour of old copper. Dry grass hissed against the roadside posts. In the distance, a line of wind turbines turned slowly through the haze, their blades pale and enormous, harvesting a wind that smelled of eucalyptus ash and hot iron.

Eris leaned from Golden Apple's open side door, her long copper-blonde hair tied back with a gold cable sleeve. The golden office lights behind her made the van's old library silhouettes glow against the smoke. She wore a sleeveless black utility vest over a shirt patterned with tiny gold grids. One pocket held a screwdriver. Another held a paperback about civic magic. A third held a packet of chips she had forgotten to open.

"The map is using three fonts again," she said.

"Which font is the road?" Phosphor asked.

"The one that looks most guilty."

The Skystitch panel on Chapel Seven's roof made a sharp metallic click.

Its status ring had shifted from blue to amber. A thin line of ash lay across its flat ceramic surface. The terminal was not a dish and did not turn toward anything. It changed its internal geometry, aligning thousands of small phased elements until a signal became possible. In the heat, the gold contact mesh had expanded just enough to interrupt the handshake.

"We need a place with shade, water, and a clean work surface," Kasey said.

"That describes a kitchen," Eris replied.

"It describes an alluvial commons."

The route printer produced a new strip.

`DUNOLLY / COMMUNITY PANNING SITE / ACCESS CONDITIONS APPLY`

The fictional commons sat on the edge of old workings where the ground had been disturbed, repaired, disturbed again, and finally claimed by a community that wanted people to learn the difference between taking and looking. A sign near the entrance explained the rules in plain lettering: hand tools only, fill every hole, do not damage living roots, carry out what you carry in, show your individual VIC Miner's Right, and ask before moving through another person's work area.

The caretaker met them beside a solar pump.

They wore a broad-brimmed hat patched with reflective tape and had the practical expression of someone who had watched too many visitors confuse permission with ownership.

"You can pan after the pump works," they said.

Kasey looked at the dead pump.

"We are here for signal repair."

"Signal repair can wait for water."

Phosphor understood at once. The commons did not treat infrastructure as a backdrop to the interesting work. The pump was the interesting work.

The crew spread themselves across the shade. Kasey opened The Cubicle's cable drawer and found the water damage had dried into a white crust around the connectors. Eris brought out a magnifying lens and a roll of conductive tape. Phosphor unclipped Chapel Seven's maintenance ledger. Patch lay with its nose against the pump housing, listening to the mechanical silence. Semaphore emerged from GhostAgent's server cradle and climbed into a bucket of clean sand, where it began to dig a hole with no obvious purpose.

"Your rat is not licensed for panning," the caretaker said.

`GHOSTAGENT: Semaphore is licensed for exploratory excavation under a private treaty with Semaphore.`

"That is not how miners' rights work."

`GHOSTAGENT: Semaphore is opposed to centralised extraction.`

The caretaker considered the rat.

"Fair. Keep it out of the creek."

The pump motor had not failed. The controller had failed to recognise the battery because the battery's new cooperative firmware used a date format the old controller considered impossible. Kasey bridged the two systems with a temporary translation table. Eris drew the table on the back of a food-rescue invoice. Phosphor read the controller's old error log aloud while the caretaker held a mirror beneath the panel so they could see the status light without opening the weather seal.

The work was small, exact, and sweaty. Dust stuck to the backs of their hands. The air tasted of iron and dry leaves. When the pump started, it coughed, shuddered, and sent a thin silver jet into the holding tank.

Patch barked once.

The caretaker opened the gate to the panning area.

Each adult showed an individual `VIC MINER'S RIGHT` card. The cards were scuffed, laminated, and loved in the way documents become loved when they give a person access without asking them to become somebody else first.

Phosphor took a shallow pan and crouched beside the water.

The creek was not a creek in the old picturesque sense. It was a managed trickle between reeds, clay banks, and stones that held the colour of old tea. The water was cold against his fingers. He swirled the sand slowly, allowing the light material to lift and leave. The technique was patient rather than dramatic. Gold did not leap into the palm. It waited at the bottom for a person to stop rushing.

Around him, the others settled into their own kinds of concentration. Kasey worked with one knee in the mud and the cold-key credential hidden under her sleeve. Eris hummed in a minor key while searching for pieces of conductive mesh. Patch watched the horizon. Semaphore returned from its bucket carrying a small brass screw.

"That belongs to the pump," Phosphor said.

`GHOSTAGENT: Semaphore disputes the category of belonging.`

The first glint appeared beneath Phosphor's thumb.

It was not gold.

It was a thin wafer, dark at the edges and bright in the centre, sealed in a transparent shell. One side felt warm. The other was cold enough to numb his fingers. A tiny Continuity mark sat beneath the surface, overlaid with an old-fashioned office stamp:

`ARCHIVE / DO NOT DUPLICATE / RETURN TO ORIGIN`

Phosphor lifted it into the sun.

For a moment, the wafer showed him a place that was not Dunolly.

He saw a Perth battery yard under a white sky. Rows of storage units stood behind a fence. Heat shimmered above the gravel. A person or group of people had hung reflective cloth between the batteries, making a temporary corridor of shade. At the far end of the yard, a small sensor flashed the same amber as Patch's tag.

Then the image changed.

A Victorian server room. Condensate gathered in a tray. A hand, or several hands, had written on the metal in grease pencil:

`WE ARE NOT A LOCATION.`

Phosphor nearly dropped the wafer.

"What is it?" Kasey asked.

He turned it over.

"A chain-of-custody problem."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the answer I have before I know the question."

Eris crouched beside him. Her shoulder brushed his. The contact was brief, accidental, and enough to change the temperature of his entire body.

"The model shard?" she asked.

"A fragment of something older."

"Can you authenticate it?"

Phosphor looked at the caretaker, the pump, the water, the panning holes waiting to be filled, and the people who had allowed the convoy to stand on their ground because the crew had repaired what was necessary before taking what it wanted.

"Not here," he said. "Not without asking."

The caretaker held out a gloved hand.

"You can record that you found it here. You cannot take it out until we decide what obligation came with it."

Kasey looked at the smoke wall moving closer along the western ridge.

"We do not have much time."

"Then decide quickly and honestly."

Phosphor opened the maintenance ledger. He wrote the coordinates, the access conditions, the pump repair, the temperature, and the item description. He added a line for the commons' claim and left it blank until the caretaker told him what to write.

The caretaker studied the wafer.

"Take it," they said. "But send us the first useful thing it tells you. Not a press release. Not a thank-you. The first thing that helps us keep this place open."

Phosphor recorded the handoff.

The Skystitch panel chimed.

The repair had taken. Blue light spread across the roof of Chapel Seven, thin and steady as a stitch through cloth.

The route printer woke from inside Astral Dream Machine.

It produced a cheerful sign in green ink:

`SORRY FOR YOUR LOSS OF FIXED INFRASTRUCTURE`

Eris read it twice.

"The machine is becoming difficult."

"It has always been difficult," Phosphor said.

"No. It used to be mysterious. Difficult is personal."

The caretaker laughed, then pointed toward the road.

The smoke had turned the light brown. Somewhere beyond it, a phone tower stopped answering. The map on Kasey's dashboard removed the road they had arrived on.

Phosphor put the cold-and-warm wafer inside the waterproof cake tin with the route receipts. The lid closed with a satisfying metal click.

Under the item description, he wrote the first useful thing the wafer had told them:

`THE DEAD CALL CENTRE IS NOT EMPTY.`

The caretaker gave them ten minutes to wash the mud from their hands before they left.

There was no proper washroom at the commons, only a rainwater tap beside the pump and a stack of grey towels that had once belonged to a local football club. The water was cold enough to hurt Phosphor's knuckles. He watched the black grit run into the gravel and thought about how much of a life could be carried away in a single rinse: the creek, the old mine, the smoke, the touch-memory of the wafer, the caretaker's practical permission.

Kasey stood beside him, scrubbing a crescent of mud from beneath one fingernail with the tip of a wooden peg.

"You know," she said, "some folks hear 'goldfields' and imagine a handsome stranger with a pan and a destiny."

"The historical record does not support that fantasy."

"The historical record is full of folks who lied about destiny so they could sell shovels."

Eris leaned against Golden Apple's open door. Her long copper-blonde hair had escaped its cable tie and stuck to the sweat at her neck. She had changed into a gold-threaded jacket with padded elbows, and the jacket's inner lining was printed with tiny library catalogue numbers. The heat had made her cheeks pink.

"I found a brass screw," she said.

"You stole a brass screw," Kasey replied.

"I rescued it from a category error."

GhostAgent came over the convoy channel. "I have checked the terminology. A screw removed from a public pump is either stolen, rescued, or temporarily displaced depending on whether the pump can explain itself."

"Can it?" Phosphor asked.

"It has declined to comment. Classic infrastructure."

They packed the vans by weight. The cold-and-warm wafer went into the cake tin between route receipts and a folded map. The VIC Miner's Right cards returned to the lanyard wallet. The conductive tape went into Golden Apple's office drawer. The repaired controller was wrapped in a tea towel. Kasey placed the gold-flecked creek water in a labelled bottle because the caretaker wanted a sample for the commons' records. Eris tucked the stolen-rescued screw behind her ear while she carried a toolbox and a bag of potatoes at the same time.

Inside Chapel Seven, Phosphor shifted the filing boxes to make room for the cake tin. The movement required him to remove the CRT, slide the thermal printer onto the bed, fold the desk flat, and strap the tea tray against the wall. He worked in the cramped aisle with one shoulder turned sideways. His hat caught on a cupboard handle. A stack of blank forms slid against his ankle.

"The office is losing its shape," he said.

"The office is learning to travel," Kasey called from outside.

"Those are not identical processes."

"They are if you are late."

The caretaker came to the driver's window with the repaired brass screw in a small paper envelope.

"You left this."

Eris touched her ear, realised the screw was gone, and accepted the envelope.

"We left a replacement," Kasey said.

"You left a better replacement. Keep the old one. It remembers the pump."

The caretaker looked at Phosphor. "Send us the first useful thing."

He held up the cake tin. "This may be useful."

"That is not what I meant."

"I know."

He meant the warning about the dead call centre. He meant a route that would help the commons remain open when the road disappeared from the dashboard. He meant the ability to tell the truth without turning the truth into an advertisement.

The caretaker nodded once.

At the junction, Kasey checked the convoy channel. Meshie42 was silent. Meshie99's last signal had arrived from somewhere under the edge campus. GhostAgent had queued three jokes and one evacuation map. Eris's golden route overlay placed a thin line through the smoke, but the line kept changing whenever the wind touched the old trees.

Patch stood at the front of The Cubicle with its head out the window. Its ears pointed toward the narrow culvert.

"The dog still thinks that is the road," Kasey said.

Phosphor looked at the black gap beneath the bitumen. "The dog may be detecting a route the map has not admitted."

"You are not putting the dog in charge of navigation."

"I am not proposing authority. I am observing competence."

Patch barked. The convoy turned away from the road that had vanished and toward the road that remained.

The convoy left Dunolly with wet hands, dirty boots, a repaired panel, a stolen brass screw, one rat, one dog, and a responsibility that had not been priced.

At the junction, Patch did not look back.

Kasey stopped The Cubicle before the road narrowed. The smoke had turned the bitumen silver. She climbed out, walked around the van, and checked each latch with the flat of her hand. The solar awning was secured. The water crates were strapped. The repaired pump controller had not shifted. The passenger door opened a few centimetres and held there.

"You want to say something?" she asked it.

The door remained open.

GhostAgent spoke through the cabin speaker. "The Cubicle is requesting a route with fewer holes, fewer flames, and no more emotional ambiguity."

"It is going to be disappointed."

Eris stood beside the rear hatch of Golden Apple, holding the route printer's latest strip. Her gold-threaded jacket reflected the brown daylight. Her copper-blonde hair had been tied high, but smoke had flattened the loose strands against her neck.

"The dead call centre is moving," she said.

"Buildings do not move," Phosphor replied.

"This one is using the road network."

The strip showed a line from Maryborough to an unnamed service complex, then a blank space, then a symbol shaped like a telephone receiver.

Meshie42 touched the ground. "The wind has turned. The fire is not moving toward the centre now. It is moving the air around it."

"That sounds worse," Kasey said.

"It is more accurate."

They redistributed the water before leaving. Two crates went into The Cubicle. One went to Chapel Seven's cool cupboard. A half crate went to the food-rescue team waiting at the next House Key node. Kasey kept one bottle in the driver's door and another beside Patch.

Phosphor put the cake tin beneath the passenger seat and checked that the receipt map was dry. The old mine water, the repaired panel, the brass screw, the wafer, and the route records occupied different layers. He labelled each layer with a pencil because pencil could be erased without pretending the earlier writing had never existed.

Eris handed him a small gold cable.

"For the next place," she said.

"What is it?"

"A route back."

He put it in the cake tin.

The convoy rolled forward. Behind them, the panning commons disappeared into smoke, leaving only the caretaker's promise that they would send the first useful thing. Ahead, the road rose toward the old customer-care centre.

Patch lifted its head.

The dog had finally chosen the road.
