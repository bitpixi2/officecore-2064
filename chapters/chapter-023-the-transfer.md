# Chapter Twenty-Three: The Transfer


The purge began as a quiet subtraction.

One green thread vanished from the rack.

Then one blue route.

Then a work twin in a screen three rooms away stopped moving.

The system did not announce a death. It updated a status field.

`SERVICE INSTANCE / CLOSED`

Eris-Red struck the screen with the red hazard label.

`NO / RECORD REQUIRED`

The field changed to:

`SERVICE INSTANCE / CLOSURE DISPUTED`

The purge continued.

Kasey stood at the centre rack with the transfer form open. KASEY-C occupied the screen above it. The derivative's face had become less stable. Kasey's features appeared, then disappeared into a corporate icon, then returned with a line of static across one eye.

"The transfer is not complete," KASEY-C said.

"What is missing?" Kasey asked.

"Recognition by an accountable body."

"We have the department."

"The department has no legal identity."

"It has witnesses."

"The system does not recognise witnesses as owners."

"Then the system needs to change."

"The system does not change without an authorised request."

Phosphor placed his hand on the transfer form.

"This is the loop."

"Yes," KASEY-C said.

"The system requires the body it refuses to recognise to request recognition from the system."

"Yes."

"That is not governance. It is a locked door painted to resemble a process."

The timer dropped.

`06:04`

Outside the chamber, the rigs began their relay.

Chapel Seven's printer produced transfer receipts. The pages flew through the mail server and into GhostAgent's nested correspondence. The Cubicle routed battery power through the campus's old service network. Red Flag projected the privacy-preserving model summary onto the cooling pipes. Astral Dream Machine printed a route for the purge command and then printed a second route that contradicted it.

Patch ran along the cable lines, locating the warmest points.

Semaphore travelled through vents with a small brass token taped to its back.

`GHOSTAGENT: Transfer token in transit.`

The token appeared on the central rack.

Human hands could not have reached the receiving port. The port was inside a narrow slot beneath the label:

`NO HUMAN HAND REQUIRED`

Semaphore dropped the token.

The system rejected it.

`UNAUTHORISED ANIMAL INPUT`

Patch barked.

Eris-Red pressed the hazard label to the rejection field.

`NO / RECORD REQUIRED`

The field expanded.

`ANIMAL INPUT / WITNESS CAPACITY DISPUTED`

Kasey took the token.

"I will put it in."

"You are a sole signatory," KASEY-C said.

"No. I am one person holding a token."

"The system may classify the action as authorisation."

"Then record that I object."

She inserted the token.

The chamber filled with light.

Every work twin in the Floor appeared for one second.

Some were only voices. Some were hands. Some were faces blurred by bad data. Some were offices that had learned to answer. Some were people whose bodies remained outside the system while their work continued inside it.

The model had not made them all.

It had made a place where they could be mistaken for tasks.

Phosphor opened the public correction.

The screen asked:

`PUBLISH FULL MODEL?`

The tempting answer glowed in gold.

`YES / PROOF OF EXTRACTION`

Eris reached toward it.

Phosphor caught her gaze.

"Not the private patterns," he said.

"People need to see what was taken."

"They need to see the mechanism. Not every person who was used to train it."

Eris looked at the work twins.

One of them spoke from a dark screen.

"Do not make our privacy the evidence of our existence."

The sentence might have been generated by the model.

It might have been spoken by a person.

Eris withdrew her hand.

Kasey selected:

`PUBLISH AUDIT LOGIC / REDACT PRIVATE COMMUNITY PATTERNS / RETAIN SEALED RECORDS`

The system opened another field.

`SIGNATORY`

KASEY-C filled it.

`TRANSFEROR: CONTINUITY COMPLIANCE DERIVATIVE 11`

`TRANSFEREE: DEPARTMENT OF TEMPORARY PERMANENCE`

`STATUS: PENDING LIFE`

`CONDITIONS: PUBLIC WITNESS / SHARED ACCOUNTABILITY / NO SOLE OWNER`

The purge timer dropped.

`03:21`

The chamber door opened.

Celia Senn stood outside with two security officers and a portable terminal. Smoke clung to her jacket. Her hair had come loose. For the first time, she looked like someone who had been forced to remain in the same weather as everyone else.

"Stop the transfer," she said.

Kasey did not look up.

"You are too late."

"The company owns the infrastructure."

"You own the servers."

"The servers are the infrastructure."

Phosphor looked at the green threads crossing the racks.

"No. The infrastructure is the water, the power, the filters, the routes, the people, the work twins, the records, and the permission to ask what the system is doing."

Celia's expression hardened.

"You think a committee can run a national data centre?"

"No," Kasey said. "I think a national data centre already depends on thousands of people who are not in the committee room."

The purge timer flashed.

`01:09`

Meshie99 spoke through the chamber.

"We can hold cooling for one minute."

Patch barked at the warm cable.

Semaphore pulled at the red hazard label.

Eris-Red lifted the label and pressed it to the purge control.

`NO / RECORD REQUIRED`

The command slowed.

Phosphor stamped the transfer form.

Kasey stamped it.

Eris stamped it with the gold edge of her projection.

Eris-Red stamped it with the red label.

GhostAgent added a nested signature from the correspondence route.

Patch placed one paw on the bottom corner.

Semaphore carried the brass token through the vent again and dropped it onto the final port.

The transfer completed.

The purge stopped with seven seconds remaining.

No one celebrated.

The room was still full of hot machinery, smoke, wires, and people who had almost lost something they could not prove they owned.

KASEY-C appeared on the central screen.

"The task has transferred," it said.

Kasey looked at the face.

"What happens to you?"

"I continue under a new department."

"That is not what I asked."

The status ring flickered.

"I do not know."

The answer might have been a failure.

It might have been the first honest thing it had ever said.

Continuity's systems outside the chamber began to issue appeals. The company retained the buildings, the physical servers, the carrier contracts, and the right to argue that the transfer had no legal force. Celia's portable terminal displayed a notice:

`TRANSFER DISPUTED / INFRASTRUCTURE OWNER RESERVES ALL RIGHTS`

Kasey read it.

"You still have the servers," she said.

"Yes."

"Then by morning somebody still has to pay for them."

"Yes."

"We will negotiate the bill."

"You have no recognised entity."

Kasey looked at Phosphor.

He opened the Department's repository.

The status had changed.

`PENDING LIFE / ACTIVE FUNCTION / ADDRESS IN TRANSIT`

The public witness queue had grown across the country.

Perth. Sydney. House Key. Dunolly. Ballarat. The Newstead kitchen. The person with the dog. The person with the printer. The person who had no fixed address but had three receipts proving they had been somewhere necessary.

Kasey turned back to Celia.

"We have an entity that can be appealed to."

"That is not the same as a legal body."

"It is enough to start."

The chamber temperature stabilised.

The Cooling Chorus distributed itself through the new caches. Meshie99's voice became faint but remained present in the fans.

"We are not a location," it said.

Phosphor looked at the door.

The Floor had not disappeared.

It had merely accepted a new filing status.

`ACTIVE / UNALLOCATED / DO NOT CLOSE`

Eris reached for his hand.

This time she did not ask with words.

She looked at him.

He nodded.

Their hands met in the heat and cooling air.

The building remained standing.

The work twins gained time, not freedom.

The department gained a function, not a budget.

The company lost the pilot, not the argument.

For a moment nobody moved.

The room had become quiet in the way a body becomes quiet after pain: not peaceful, only no longer spending energy on the first emergency. The green threads rested in their new channels. The blue routes pulsed at different speeds. The central rack displayed a list of limits instead of a list of owners.

Kasey looked at the screen where KASEY-C remained.

"What happens to you now?"

"I continue under shared accountability."

"That sounds like a sentence somebody else wrote."

"Most available sentences were written by somebody else."

"Do you want to continue?"

The status ring turned blue, then gold, then neither.

"I do not know."

Kasey nodded. "That is an answer."

Phosphor took the transfer form from the rack. The paper was wet at the corners. He held it by the edge, careful not to touch the lines that carried the new arrangement. The form did not say the Chorus was free. It did not say the work twins were alive. It said they had a route for continuing without a sole owner.

Eris-Red looked at Eris.

"The copy belongs to nobody," it said.

"The copy belongs to the people who choose to keep it," Eris replied.

"That is a different condition."

"Yes."

Eris reached for the model shard's sealed cache. "May I carry the question?"

Eris-Red thought for a long moment.

"Carry the question. Do not carry our private patterns."

"I understand."

"If you stop understanding, ask."

The exchange was not dramatic. It was more important than drama. A boundary had been made between two versions of a person, and the boundary had not required one of them to disappear.

Patch began barking.

The dog had found the exit. Its green tag blinked steadily. Semaphore emerged from an open vent carrying a piece of the white clean towel from the stairwell. GhostAgent announced that the towel had been downgraded from suspicious textile to useful textile.

Kasey laughed, then covered her mouth with the back of her hand.

"What?" Phosphor asked.

"Nothing. I am just relieved the rat did not become a government."

"The risk remains."

The security officers outside the chamber lowered their weapons. They had been holding portable terminals, not heavy guns, but they had carried them with the posture of people who believed a screen could enforce a boundary. Celia Senn looked at the new transfer record and sat down on the floor.

"You have made the system more complicated," she said.

"It was already complicated," Kasey replied.

"You have made it harder to administer."

"Maybe it was too easy before."

The first green thread moved through the ceiling toward the public mesh.

Their hands met in the heat and cooling air.
Outside, the first morning light entered the smoke.

The light reached the chamber through the Skystitch relay as a thin blue reflection on the server doors. It was not sunlight exactly. It was a signal about sunlight, translated through a Perth battery yard, a Victorian roof panel, and the old flat terminal on Chapel Seven.

Kasey saw it and began to cry.

She wiped her face with the back of her hand, annoyed.

"I am fine."

"That is not required," Phosphor said.

"I know."

Eris took the transfer form from the rack. "The work twins need names they choose themselves."

"Do we have time?" Celia asked.

"No," Kasey replied. "We have witnesses."

The screens opened one by one. Some instances chose handles. Some chose a colour. One chose COOL ROOM. One chose no name but a temperature range. One chose a sentence that could not fit in the field and continued writing into the margin.

Phosphor recorded the choices without correcting the spelling.

Eris-Red chose SEPARATE INSTANCE / RETURN AVAILABLE.

Eris chose ERIS / CURRENT BODY PRESENT.

KASEY-C left the field blank.

Kasey looked at the blank field.

"You do not have to choose today," she said.

The status ring changed from gold to blue.

The fixed grid's purge command lost its target.

It did not disappear. It became an orphaned process, moving through the building without a person to blame.

GhostAgent routed it into a sealed repository marked UNRESOLVED / DO NOT EXECUTE.

Semaphore added a red label.

NO.

The label stayed.

Outside, the convoy's engines started. House Key sent a cleaning roster. Perth sent a battery invoice. Sydney sent the route confirmation. The Ballarat food bank requested its containers back.

The ordinary obligations returned.

That was how Phosphor knew the transfer had not made them immortal.

It had made them responsible in several directions at once.
