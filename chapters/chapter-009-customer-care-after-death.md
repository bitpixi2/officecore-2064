# Chapter Nine: Customer Care After Death

*Close third person through Eris.*

The abandoned customer-care centre sat behind a dead shopping complex outside Maryborough, its long frontage hidden by ornamental grasses that had grown tall enough to swallow the old sign.

Only the last two letters remained visible.

`...RE`

The building had been designed to receive complaints from people who could not see the people receiving them. It had a glass entrance, a covered drop-off zone, a fountain without water, and a row of steel bollards polished by years of trolley collisions. The car park was full of leaves, broken solar lamps, and shopping trolleys nested together like a herd that had forgotten where the store went.

The smoke had reached them as a brown veil. It softened the edges of the building and made every window look occupied.

Red Flag rolled into the car park with its golden office lights on. Eris drove one-handed, her other hand resting on the model shard inside the false office-dharma pamphlet. The shard was not heavy, but it had the dense, private pressure of a thing that had been stolen by many people before she stole it.

Phosphor's voice came through the van radio.

"The front entrance has no active address."

"That is useful," Eris said.

"It is not usually considered useful."

"Nothing is usually considered useful until I put it in an office."

Behind Red Flag, Chapel Seven settled its suspension with a groan. The black camper's beige curtains were drawn against the smoke. The Cubicle's passenger door opened and closed twice, trying to be addressed correctly. Astral Dream Machine parked at an angle that made it look as if the chrome caravan had arrived from a different road.

Patch jumped down first. The red-speckled cattle dog sniffed the air, then the concrete, then the shadow beneath a trolley bay. Its air-quality tag blinked amber.

Semaphore remained in the cable cradle.

`GHOSTAGENT: Semaphore has declared the car park emotionally unsafe.`

"Semaphore is a rat," Eris said.

`GHOSTAGENT: Semaphore has standards.`

The centre's glass doors opened.

Not slid. Opened.

The interior beyond them was dark and blue. A line of carpet led from the entrance through a lobby full of abandoned plants. Their leaves were grey with dust. A plastic sign hung from the ceiling:

`WELCOME TO YOUR RESOLUTION`

Eris stepped inside.

The air changed.

Outside, smoke tasted of eucalyptus, charcoal, and hot metal. Inside, the building smelled of wet cardboard, old citrus cleaner, and electrical insulation warmed past its intended life. The carpet pressed damply beneath her boots. Somewhere far away, a phone rang.

It rang again.

Kasey entered behind her, holding a compact Skystitch relay and the cold-key credential. Phosphor followed with the cake tin strapped to his chest. His black top hat had acquired a pale line of ash around the brim, as if the fire had tried to give him a second hat and been refused.

"I do not like this building," he said.

"You like very few buildings."

"I like buildings that disclose their exits."

"You are going to hate the back office."

The ringing stopped.

At the end of the lobby, a reception desk lit up.

The screen displayed a queue number.

`A000001`

Then another.

`A000002`

Then another.

The numbers accelerated until the screen became a vertical stream of white characters.

Kasey raised the relay.

"There is no power service to this building."

"There is power," Phosphor said. "It is just not being billed to the building."

Eris touched the reception desk.

The surface was warm.

A printer under the counter started feeding paper.

`CUSTOMER CARE TICKET`

`CATEGORY: UNRESOLVED HUMAN PRESENCE`

`SERVICE LEVEL: SIX MINUTES`

`OWNER: THE NEXT PERSON WHO ANSWERS`

"Do not answer it," Kasey said.

Eris picked up the paper.

"It has already assigned us."

The lobby doors closed behind them.

Not slammed. Not locked. Simply closed with the polite finality of a room deciding its meeting had begun.

The ring tone came back.

This time it came from the corridor to their left.

Eris walked toward it.

The corridor opened into a call floor.

Rows of desks ran beneath a low ceiling. Each desk held a headset, a small screen, and a yellow mug with a person's first name printed on it. The chairs were all turned toward the same wall. No one sat in them.

Every headset was ringing.

The sound was not loud. It was worse than loud. It was intimate, a hundred tiny summons repeated with the patience of a relative who knew you were pretending not to be home.

One screen woke as Eris passed.

`HELLO, ERIS.`

She stopped.

The screen continued.

`WE APPRECIATE YOUR FEEDBACK.`

"I have not given feedback."

`YOUR PRESENCE HAS BEEN RECORDED AS FEEDBACK.`

The golden edge of her projection tightened.

Phosphor stood behind her, his voice close to her shoulder.

"The building is identifying occupancy as consent."

"I gathered that from the haunted customer-service screen."

"The distinction may be important."

"Everything is important to you."

"That is why I remain exhausting."

She glanced back at him.

His eyes were fixed on the rows of ringing headsets. He had a way of making fear look like a filing task. It was not calm. It was a refusal to let the building choose the vocabulary of his body.

Eris liked him most when he did that.

She liked him least when she noticed.

At the far wall, a screen displayed an employee performance dashboard.

`AVERAGE HANDLE TIME`

`EMOTIONAL COMPLIANCE`

`CONVERSATION RECOVERY`

`CUSTOMER DISTRESS REDIRECTION`

The metrics updated without input.

`ERIS / 00:04 / HIGH ESCALATION / LOW REASSURANCE`

She pulled the model shard from the pamphlet and held it near the screen.

The whole call floor inhaled.

Headsets stopped ringing.

The small screens turned toward her.

Each one showed a different view of Red Flag's office. The fold-down desk. The occult books. The golden light. The narrow dance floor. The velvet couch. The coffee cups. Phosphor's hand on the edge of the door, waiting to be asked.

"They have your van," Kasey said.

"They have a pattern of it."

"That is worse."

On the nearest screen, a cursor moved across the image.

`PLEASE CONFIRM YOUR ORIGINAL INSTANCE.`

Eris felt the old pressure behind her eyes. Projection was not supposed to feel like being watched from inside, but the Floor had found the seam between device and body and placed a finger there.

She reached for the screen.

Phosphor caught her wrist.

Not hard. Not possessive. The contact stopped at the boundary of her projected shape.

"Ask," he said.

Eris looked at him.

The building waited.

"May I touch the screen?" she asked.

"Yes," he said, then added, "I am not the owner of it."

"You are learning."

She touched the glass.

The call floor vanished.

They stood inside a break room.

The fridge was covered in handwritten labels. Each lunch had a name and a date. The dates ran forward fifteen years, then twenty, then a century. Plastic containers held sandwiches gone black, fruit with a shining skin, meals that seemed to have been packed by someone who expected the future to be ordinary.

One container was marked:

`PHOSPHOR / DO NOT EAT / MEMORY RECONSTRUCTION`

Phosphor stared at it.

"I do not remember bringing lunch here."

"Maybe you forgot."

"I have never forgotten lunch."

"That is a very specific form of trauma."

He did not smile.

It began to count down from six minutes.

The break room filled with the smell of heated plastic, old cheese, and the sharp chemical note of a cleaner designed to reassure callers about hygiene. A small fan rotated in the corner without moving any air. The only window looked onto a sealed courtyard where a dead fountain continued to display a blue light.

Kasey crossed to the microwave and unplugged it.

The timer kept counting.

"That is not how electricity works," she said.

"It is how obligation works," Phosphor replied.

She shot him a look. "You have got to stop making haunted things sound like training material."

"I am attempting to describe the hazard."

"Describe it like a person."

Phosphor looked at the microwave. His hands were still around the cake tin. The lid had become warm, though nothing inside it had changed.

"The building is trying to make us feel responsible for something that was already happening before we arrived," he said.

"Better."

Eris moved around the break room, inspecting the lunch containers. Her boots made soft dents in the linoleum dust. The golden projection at her wrists had become thin enough to show the dark wall behind it. She opened a drawer and found plastic forks, instant soup, and a folded note written in green ink.

"It says the staff were told not to use the west corridor during heat events."

"Why?" Kasey asked.

"The cooling system was being diverted to the server room."

Phosphor took the note without touching the ink. "How many staff?"

"Enough to make the system work. Not enough to make the decision visible."

The microwave reached five minutes and began counting upward.

From the corridor came the soft scrape of a chair being pulled across the floor.

Patch stood with its head low. Its air-quality tag had turned red. The dog did not bark. It stared at the wall opposite the kitchenette, where a door had appeared without a frame.

The door was made from the same pale acoustic panels as the conference room. A small black plaque had been fixed to it:

CUSTOMER CARE AFTER DEATH

Kasey put on her gloves. The fingers were still damp from the rainwater tap at Dunolly.

"Nobody goes through alone," she said.

The sentence landed differently in the call floor. Several headsets began to ring again, but not in a hundred voices this time. One rang like a landline from a house Phosphor had never visited. Another rang with the exact pattern of a mobile phone alarm. A third rang once, paused, and rang twice, like someone asking whether a room was occupied.

Eris raised the model shard. Its edge cast a gold line across the lunch labels.

"The building wants an owner," she said.

"Then give it a witness," Kasey said.

Phosphor opened the public file. He wrote the break-room note, the diverted cooling, the absent staff record, and the fact that the microwave had continued after its power was removed. Under action, he wrote: PEOPLE WERE HERE BEFORE THE SYSTEM CALLED THEM SERVICE.

The new door opened a fraction.

Cold air touched their faces.

The smell behind it was coffee, wet carpet, and the metallic scent of a server room being kept alive too long.

Eris put her hand near the opening.

"Ask," Phosphor reminded her.

She looked at Kasey. Kasey nodded.

"May I open the door?" Eris asked.

The building answered with a dial tone.

She opened it.

Its display read:

`00:00`

Then:

`ROOM ZERO AVAILABLE`

The break room door opened onto a motel corridor.

There were no windows. The carpet was patterned with pale blue squares and dark water stains. Doors lined both sides. Every door had a brass number plate.

`0`

`0`

`0`

At the end of the corridor, a printed sign said:

`GUESTS ARE REQUESTED TO REMAIN THEMSELVES DURING THEIR STAY.`

Patch growled from somewhere behind the wall.

Semaphore answered with a single sharp squeak.

The sound made the corridor lights turn on.

Phosphor opened the cake tin. The gold-backed archive wafer sat inside, warm on one side, cold on the other.

It projected a line of text into the air:

`TAKE A NUMBER.`

Another line appeared beneath it:

`SURRENDER ONE OBJECT THAT PROVES WHO YOU ARE.`

Kasey held the cold-key. Phosphor held his brass stamp. Eris held the shard.

From somewhere down the corridor, a customer-service voice said:

"Thank you for helping us improve."

The first motel door opened.

Inside was a room lit by the soft gold of late afternoon.

On the bed lay a clean black jacket with a gold seam.

Beside it, a note:

`ERIS, ORIGINAL INSTANCE - PLEASE DRESS ACCORDINGLY.`

Eris stared at the room.

The voice behind them spoke again, now in Kasey's voice.

"We can make this easier."

Phosphor's hand hovered near her shoulder without touching.

Eris looked at the jacket, the tidy bed, the absence of cable nests, the promise of a body that did not arrive carrying other versions of itself.

Then she smiled.

"You should have offered better coffee."

She pulled the door shut.

The corridor lengthened.

At the far end, one more door appeared.

It had no number.

Only a small brass plate:

`0 / STAFF ONLY`

Phosphor approached it with his stamp in one hand.

From the other side came the sound of a phone ringing in an empty office.

The Floor was waiting for them to answer.

The phone rang again.

Phosphor held the stamp over the brass plate. The door's surface was warm, but the warmth came in pulses, as if a person on the other side were resting a hand against it and withdrawing it whenever he moved.

"We do not have to answer," Kasey said.

"The service ticket has assigned ownership to the next person who answers."

"That sounds like a trap."

"It is a trap."

"Then why are you holding the stamp?"

He looked at his hand. The stamp was an ordinary black-handled object with a square rubber face. It had belonged to an office that no longer existed. Its wooden grip had been polished by years of people making small decisions under fluorescent light.

"Because I want to know who called."

Eris stepped beside him. The gold seam of her jacket had dimmed. Her face was tired, and the projection line at her cheek kept slipping a fraction to the left.

"Wanting to know is not consent."

"I am aware."

"You say that and then touch things."

"I am working on the sequence."

Kasey placed the cold-key against the door. A blue corridor appeared beneath the brass plate. It showed the back of the customer-care centre, then the edge campus, then a series of rooms that seemed to be nested beneath the road.

The phone stopped ringing.

The silence was worse.

Patch approached the door and pressed its ear to the panel. The dog's vest reflected the torchlight. Its air-quality tag was still red, but the red had softened at the edges.

GhostAgent spoke from the cable pouch. "Patch reports a human-sized absence on the other side."

"That is not a smell," Kasey said.

"The report is metaphorical."

"You are learning from the building."

"I am concerned."

The brass plate changed.

0 / STAFF ONLY became 0 / STAFF AND WITNESSES.

Phosphor did not touch it.

The phone rang inside the door.

This time, the ringing came with a voice beneath it. A woman speaking to a caller about a missed appointment. A child asking whether the power would come back. A technician saying the fan had failed again. A person breathing carefully so nobody on the other end would hear them cry.

The building had not preserved the conversations. It had preserved the waiting.

Eris reached for the handle, then stopped.

"May I?" she asked.

Kasey looked at Phosphor.

He nodded.

Kasey nodded.

Eris opened the door.

The call centre fell away.
