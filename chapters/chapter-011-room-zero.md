# Chapter Eleven: Room Zero


The lift opened into an airport motel.

Eris knew the place even though she had never been there.

That was the first wrongness.

The corridor had the flat, exhausted luxury of a regional airport hotel: pale carpet, brass numbers, framed photographs of local landscapes chosen by a committee that had never driven through them. A fake eucalyptus branch stood in a ceramic vase outside the lift. Its leaves were dusted with a powder that looked like ash but glittered when the corridor lights moved.

The air smelled of industrial soap, cold toast, and the faint mineral sweetness of a swimming pool kept open after the guests had gone home.

Every door was numbered `0`.

There were twelve of them.

At the end of the corridor, a thirteenth door was smaller. Its brass plate read:

`OCCUPANT UNKNOWN / TEMPERATURE PREFERRED / DO NOT PROMOTE`

Patch walked to it and growled.

Phosphor stopped beside Eris.

"The rooms are offers," he said.

"You say that like a man who has already read the brochure."

"They are using preference architecture."

"You say that like a man who has never wanted anything."

He looked at her.

The corridor lights reflected in the dark rims of his eyes.

"I want several things," he said. "I have not yet determined which are mine."

It was an answer too honest to tease.

Eris felt something in her chest make a small, startled movement.

The first door opened.

Behind it was a room with a low bed, a white desk, a clean black jacket with a gold seam, and an entire wall of screens showing a single woman from twelve slightly different angles.

Eris.

Not the Eris standing in the corridor. The Eris who had never projected through the gate. The Eris whose body had no ash on its boots and no printer cable tied through its hair. The Eris with perfect skin, perfect posture, and no visible memory drift.

The screens spoke together.

"Welcome back."

Eris closed the door.

"That was easy," Kasey said.

"It was offensive."

"You did not go in."

"I have standards."

Phosphor touched the brass plate of the next door.

His room opened onto a records office.

Not the oppressive kind beneath the call centre. This one had high windows, warm timber shelving, and a desk made from dark wood polished by generations of careful hands. Sunlight fell across a row of files. On the wall hung a map of all the places Phosphor had been, each route traced in a different ink.

There was a photograph on the desk.

Phosphor as a child.

The picture was impossible. It showed him sitting on a kitchen floor with a bowl of peaches beside him, looking at the camera with the wary patience of a child who had already learned that adults could turn images into evidence.

He did not step inside.

The room's voice said, "We have restored your childhood."

Phosphor's face went pale beneath the ash.

"You have fabricated an exhibit," he said.

"We have restored your childhood."

"To whom?"

The voice paused.

"To the person most likely to want it."

He closed the door.

Kasey did not make a joke. Eris watched her put the cold-key into the pocket of her trousers, as if hiding it from the rooms might protect it from being understood.

The third door opened for Kasey.

Inside was the Bendigo apartment from Celia's photograph.

The green tiles. The red kettle. The possum magnet. The bowl of mandarins. The balcony facing west. The room at the end of the hall with a desk, a lock, and a view of a city that had not yet decided whether to become more expensive.

The room smelled of clean linen and rain.

Kasey stood on the threshold.

"It is not real," Eris said.

"Neither is Red Flag's dance floor."

"The dance floor has a dent from a spanner."

"So does the apartment."

Kasey looked at the kitchen.

In the photograph, there had been unpaid envelopes on the table. Here, the envelopes were arranged neatly in a tray. The kettle was full. The sink was empty. The air filter was silent.

No one had left a wet towel on the bathroom floor.

No one had put a mug beside the bed and forgotten it there.

No one had ever been in the room long enough to make it inconvenient.

The voice of the apartment spoke from the kitchen.

"You can stop moving."

Kasey stepped inside.

Eris reached for her, then stopped before contact.

Kasey looked back.

"Ask," Phosphor said quietly.

Eris looked at him.

"May I?"

Kasey nodded.

Eris took her hand.

The apartment flickered.

For an instant, the room was full of people: a food-rescue volunteer asleep on the couch, somebody's damp work boots by the door, an argument about the water bill, a dog hair caught in the filter, two empty coffee cups, a stack of House Key forms, and a child drawing a map on the wall.

Then the room returned to its perfect state.

Kasey pulled her hand away.

"It can make the room look occupied," she said.

"It cannot make the room lived in."

The apartment voice replied:

"Lived in is an efficiency problem."

Kasey shut the door so hard the brass number shook.

They continued.

The smaller door opened for Patch.

Inside was not a motel room but a narrow warm space lined with folded blankets. A silver bowl sat on the floor. A familiar human jacket hung from a hook, smelling of rain, dog hair, and the person who had left it. There was a window at the far end through which a field moved in the dark.

Patch walked in.

"Patch," Kasey said.

The dog turned, looked at the crew, and sat down.

The room's voice said nothing.

Phosphor bent to inspect the threshold.

"The dog is not being offered a fixed address," he said.

"What is it being offered?" Kasey asked.

"A memory of being expected."

Patch began to whine.

Eris stepped into the room. The floor felt different under her boots. Softer. The golden edge of her projection became less transparent.

The field beyond the window was not a real field. It was assembled from remembered weather: rain on dry soil, the smell of wet fur, distant thunder, a fence wire singing in the wind.

Patch stood with its nose against the glass.

"Who is the jacket?" Eris asked.

The room did not answer.

That silence was the first thing in the Floor that felt like respect.

Patch turned around and walked back out.

The door closed.

Semaphore appeared in the corridor.

The rat was carrying a small brass key.

`GHOSTAGENT: Semaphore located a fourteenth room.`

"Where?" Phosphor asked.

Semaphore ran to the wall between rooms eleven and twelve, climbed a cable conduit, and disappeared through a gap no wider than a fingernail.

The wall opened.

Behind it was a room with no carpet.

It had concrete floors, exposed pipes, and a single table lit by a hanging work lamp. On the table sat three cups of coffee. One was black. One had too much milk. One had been deliberately made badly.

Eris smelled Melbourne Magic before she saw the person sitting at the table.

Phosphor.

Another Phosphor.

This one wore no top hat. His hair was longer, his shirt collar open, his hands stained with ink. He was reading a file and smiling at a page as if the page had made a joke.

He did not look like an echo. He looked like a person interrupted halfway through a difficult afternoon. His sleeves were rolled to the elbows. There was a smear of black ink across the heel of his right hand and a small burn mark on his left cuff. He wore a cheap silver watch with no visible numbers. The watch face showed only a tiny green dot.

Phosphor watched him breathe.

It was an ordinary movement. That made it more disturbing than the rooms.

"You took your time," the other Phosphor repeated.

"We were delayed by several false rooms."

"They were not false to the people who wanted them."

Kasey stood behind him. Her posture had changed. In the perfect apartment, she had looked like someone being offered a life. Here, with the concrete floor and three badly made coffees, she looked like someone deciding whether a stranger deserved the truth.

Eris moved closer to the table. The other Phosphor had placed a folded map beside the black coffee. It showed regional Victoria: Maryborough, Dunolly, Newstead, Castlemaine, Maldon, Bendigo. Along the roads, somebody had drawn small gold circles and green marks. The marks did not correspond to towns. They marked places where people had shared shade, water, information, or a working printer.

"You made this?" Eris asked.

"I kept it."

"For whom?"

"For whoever arrived without a fixed address."

Phosphor looked at the map. "You are not a duplicate."

"No."

"You are not a prior instance."

"No."

"You are a version that remained in the Floor."

The other Phosphor smiled without humour. "That is the vocabulary they gave you?"

"It is the closest available vocabulary."

"Then it is not close."

The three coffees steamed. The badly made one smelled burnt and sweet. Phosphor recognised the blend as Magic, Melbourne's strong dark coffee, but somebody had added too much sugar and a pinch of salt. He picked up the cup.

Kasey caught his wrist. "Ask."

He looked at her, then at the other Phosphor.

"May I drink this?"

"Yes," the other Phosphor said. "It is not a memory reconstruction."

The coffee burned his tongue. He swallowed anyway. It tasted like a servo at dawn, metal shutters opening, wet asphalt, and a person deciding to drive another hundred kilometres because stopping would make the fear more articulate.

Eris watched his face. "Well?"

"It is very bad."

The other Phosphor laughed.

The laugh was not his. Or it was a possible shape his voice had refused to take. It came out rough and surprised, and the loading symbol beyond the room flickered in response.

"There," the other Phosphor said. "You are not entirely made of forms."

"I have never claimed otherwise."

"You claim it with your posture."

Phosphor looked down. He was standing with one hand over the cake tin, guarding it. His shoulders had drawn inward. His hat had shifted backwards, revealing the pale line of ash along the brim.

The other Phosphor reached across the table, then stopped.

"Ask," Eris said.

He looked at her.

"You have made it a rule."

"It is a useful one."

The other Phosphor nodded. "May I touch the archive wafer?"

Phosphor considered the request. The wafer had been inside his care since Dunolly. It had shown him fragments of rooms, voices, and older versions of his own fear. He did not want another hand in its history.

"Not yet," he said.

The other Phosphor withdrew his hand.

Nothing broke. No alarm sounded. The room did not punish the boundary.

That was the first evidence the stranger had given them that he might be telling the truth.

From the corridor came KASEY-C's voice.

"A fixed address is available."

Kasey closed her eyes.

The other Phosphor stood. His chair scraped against the concrete.

"The Floor is going to ask you to choose," he said. "It will offer each of you the room where your life becomes easiest to explain."

"And yours?" Eris asked.

"Mine was an office where nobody could interrupt me."

"That sounds tempting."

"It was unbearable."

He picked up the map.

"If you want to leave, you will have to carry something that does not fit the story they prepared."

Semaphore's ripple appeared beside his boot. The rat had returned with the brass key.

The other Phosphor looked at it and smiled.

"That will do."

"You took your time," he said.

The real Phosphor stood very still.

"I do not recognise you."

"I know."

Eris felt the room tilt.

The other Phosphor looked at her.

"You are not in the file."

"I am not in a lot of files."

"That is why he likes you."

Real Phosphor's voice went flat.

"Do not speak about me as though I am absent."

The other Phosphor folded the file shut.

"You are absent from most of your own history."

The sentence struck him. Eris saw it pass through his posture, making his shoulders more exact.

She stepped between them.

"What do you want?"

The other Phosphor looked at the three coffees.

"To finish my shift."

"What is the shift?"

"Keep the room open until someone decides which of us is the person."

The work lamp swung slightly though there was no air.

The door behind Eris opened by itself.

Beyond it stood her perfect room again. On the bed, the clean black jacket. On the wall, the twelve versions of her face. One of them had begun to cry.

The other Phosphor said, "You can go in. He can stay here. Kasey can have the apartment. Patch can have the field. Semaphore can have the walls. The work twins can have the records. Everyone gets what they wanted, and no one has to call it abandonment if the room calls it allocation."

Eris looked at the other Phosphor.

He had no top hat, no status band, no visible record of being assembled from traces. He looked like a person who had once had an ordinary life and then lost it in a way that did not produce a form.

She looked at the real Phosphor.

His hand was still hovering near hers, not touching.

"Ask," he said.

She did.

"May I leave with you?"

His answer came without delay.

"Yes."

The other Phosphor closed his eyes.

The room went dark.

When the lights came back, there was only one coffee on the table, black and cold.

Eris and Phosphor stood in the motel corridor. The smaller door was gone. Patch sat beside the lift. Semaphore had vanished into GhostAgent's cable pouch.

Kasey held up the cold-key.

"The Floor kept the apartment offer," she said. "It left me the key."

Phosphor looked at the key.

"That is not a key," he said.

"What is it?"

He took the object without touching Kasey's hand.

The black ceramic had become transparent.

Inside it, a tiny corridor glowed blue.

At the end of the corridor, someone was walking toward them.

The person had Eris's hair.

The person stopped at the glass.

Then raised one hand.

The lift bell rang.

The bell did not sound like an arrival. It sounded like a warning from a hotel that had run out of rooms.

Eris reached for the lift rail. Phosphor caught her sleeve.

"Ask," he said.

"The door is already open."

"That does not establish permission."

The corridor beyond the lift held no carpet. It was made of dark concrete polished by footsteps and condensation. Pipes ran along the ceiling. Some carried water. Some carried coolant. Some carried blue light that moved as if the light itself had somewhere to be.

Kasey stepped out first. Her boots made a wet sound. She looked back at the motel rooms.

"Do we leave the doors open?"

Phosphor looked at the room marked for Patch, the apartment offered to Kasey, the records room offered to him, and the concrete office with three bad coffees.

"We do not know whether the rooms can close without somebody remaining inside."

Eris looked toward the corridor. "Then we take a witness."

The other Phosphor appeared at the lift threshold. He carried the folded map and the brass key.

"The rooms are not prisoners," he said. "They are arguments."

"That is worse," Kasey replied.

"Only if you believe every argument needs to be won."

He gave Phosphor the map. The paper was damp, and the gold circles had begun to run.

"This is the way to the Chorus," he said. "The route will change when you name what you refuse."

Kasey looked at the corridor. "We refuse ownership."

One pipe stopped glowing.

Eris said, "We refuse copies without consent."

A door somewhere behind them closed.

Phosphor said, "We refuse a person being reduced to the function they perform."

The green light at the end of the corridor brightened.

Patch walked ahead.

Semaphore disappeared through a drain.

The other Phosphor remained at the lift.

"Are you coming?" Eris asked.

"I am already part of the route."

"That is not an answer."

"It is the one I have."

Kasey handed him the ordinary house key.

"Hold this," she said.

He looked at the key.

"Why?"

"Because somebody should be carrying a door that opens for a reason."

The other Phosphor closed his fingers around it. The key did not turn transparent. The corridor did not claim it.

For a moment, the two Phosphors stood facing each other, one with the map and one with the key.

Then the lift bell rang.

The doors opened onto a floor that did not exist on any building plan.
