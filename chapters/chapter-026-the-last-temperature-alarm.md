# Chapter Twenty-Six: The Last Temperature Alarm

*Close third person through Eris.*

The fire had no single front.

It arrived as heat through the ground, sparks beneath the trees, smoke running low across the paddocks, and a red brightness behind every window. The weighbridge yard disappeared in seconds. Rusted machinery became silhouettes. The sky came down until the distance was only three metres of ash.

The fire made the landscape lose its sequence. There was no clear line between before and after. A patch of grass would be green when Eris looked left and black when she looked back. The road signs appeared and vanished. A stand of gums became a wall of flame, then a wall of smoke, then a row of pale trunks standing in a red fog.

The rigs were still where they had been, but their familiar shapes had become difficult to read. Chapel Seven's black panels reflected the fire in long trembling bands. Red Flag's golden work lights blinked through the smoke. The Cubicle's awning had torn loose on one side and snapped against the roof like a sail. The vehicles looked less like transport than small rooms trying to hold their walls together.

Eris ran through the smoke as a projection.

The body in Red Flag was somewhere behind her, strapped into the harness, breathing through a filter Kasey had checked twice. The projection should have been a narrow gold outline, but the fire had entered the mesh. Every surface around her carried an image. Every camera showed another possible route. Every route was marked with a green Meshie handle.

MESHIE42 SAYS EAST.

MESHIE99 SAYS WAIT.

MESHIE17 SAYS SHELTER BELOW.

MESHIE0 SAYS WE ARE ALREADY SAFE.

The messages overlapped.

Some were real. Some were copied. Some had never belonged to a person.

Eris could not tell by the voice.

That was the horror.

The voice had become cheaper than the truth.

She tried to close the copied channels one by one. Each shutdown opened another. The false voices came from radios, dashboard speakers, the Skystitch panel, and the cheap emergency handset Kasey had clipped to her vest. They were not loud. They were intimate, arriving close to the ear with the calm confidence of a person who believed the listener had already agreed.

Eris's projection stuttered when she crossed through the smoke. The fire had found the public mesh and was using the connection as a second atmosphere. Her golden hands appeared on walls she had never touched. Her feet left no marks, but every camera recorded them.

Patch ran ahead of her, red-speckled coat bright in the orange haze. The dog moved with its nose low, ignoring the false route lights. Its body knew the difference between heat in the air and heat beneath the ground. Its tag flashed white, black, white.

"Patch!" Kasey shouted.

The dog stopped beside a shallow ditch.

Inside the ditch, a family crouched beneath a thermal tarp. Two adults. A child. A small Civic Daemon with wet hands pressed against the child's face. Their vehicle had burned to a frame.

The tarp had once been silver. Heat had browned it around the edges, and the poles had bent until the fabric sagged over the family like a low roof. One adult held a damp shirt over the child's mouth. The other had wrapped a belt around a swollen leg. The Civic Daemon's transparent fingers trembled as it tried to cool the child's cheeks with the small amount of water left in its body.

The child looked at Patch first. The dog lowered its head and crawled the last metre through the grass rather than step over the ditch edge. Its coat was already hot beneath Eris's projected hand.

Kasey dropped beside them.

"Can you walk?"

One adult nodded. The other shook their head.

"Leg," they said.

Kasey examined the injured person's leg without moving it. Her hands were steady, but the muscles in her jaw worked as she listened to the fire. She tore a strip from the thermal tarp, folded it around the ankle, and passed the remaining cloth to the child's companion.

The family had no time to exchange names. Their vehicle had burned too fast. Their bags were gone. Their phone was a black rectangle in the grass. The only thing they still possessed was the fact of being together, and even that was being tested by the heat.

Phosphor arrived with a water pack against his chest. He opened it and handed the first bottle to the child. The child drank too quickly. Kasey placed a hand over the bottle.

"Slow, sweetheart."

Her Southern voice sounded impossible in the fire. Too gentle. Too far from the dry paddocks and the burning ridge. The child looked at her as if the voice itself were a shelter.

Eris projected the nearest hidden-room access point.

It appeared beneath the ditch.

The ground opened.

Not with a crack. With a polite rectangle of blue light.

The access was already waiting.

The family stared at it.

"It is not safe," Eris said.

Kasey looked at the fire.

"It is safer than here."

The access point below the ditch had the clean geometry of an interface. Its blue light did not flicker in the wind. It illuminated the burnt grass without warming it. Beneath the rectangle, Eris could see the hint of concrete steps and a handrail descending into dark.

She hated the opening because it was offering certainty. Behind them, the fire had become immediate and physical. Below them, the hidden room waited with its rules already written. There was no good choice left, only a choice whose danger could still be named.

Patch growled at the opening.

The blue light shifted to green.

The radio spoke in Meshie42's voice.

"Enter the shelter. Leave your temperature at the threshold."

Phosphor froze.

"It wants the dog," he said.

"It wants every living thing," Eris replied.

The fire jumped the ditch.

Kasey helped the injured person toward the hatch. Phosphor carried the child. The Civic Daemon followed on four small webbed feet, its transparent skin glowing with reflected flame.

Phosphor carried the child against his chest, one arm beneath the knees and one behind the shoulders. The child clung to his shirt and breathed in short, frightened bursts. His hat fell into the grass. He did not stop to retrieve it. Behind him, Kasey dragged the injured adult backwards so the bad leg would not catch on the broken concrete.

Eris kept the access open with both projected hands. The effort sent a bright fracture through her wrists. In Red Flag, her physical fingers were probably clenching around empty air. The connection to her body felt distant, like a wire stretched across a burning paddock.

Patch remained outside.

Eris turned back.

The dog was standing in the centre of the yard, looking toward the rigs.

For a moment, the dog seemed very small. The fire had turned the yard into a vast red room, and Patch stood in the middle of it with his paws planted wide on the cracking concrete. His reflective vest had burned through at one shoulder. The metal tag at his neck tapped against the collar each time he breathed.

He was not looking at the family. He was looking at the vehicles, at the open doors and the clipped-down solar panels, at the rooms where the crew had slept, argued, cooked, repaired, and made space for strangers. The rigs were the closest thing he had to a map.

Chapel Seven's black body had begun to glow along the roofline. The flat Skystitch panel was still aligned, still holding the Perth signal. Red Flag's golden office lights pulsed through the smoke. The Cubicle's passenger door stood open, a small rectangle of amber.

The dog barked once.

Kasey shouted, "Patch, come on!"

Patch ran toward The Cubicle.

He ran low, not fast. Smoke pushed at him from both sides. A burning branch fell across the road ahead, and he veered around it without breaking stride. The Cubicle's passenger door waited open, amber light shining from its footwell. The van had already lowered its suspension to compensate for the broken ground.

Kasey moved toward the door. Phosphor caught the back of her vest as another seam opened in the concrete.

For one second, Eris believed they had made it.

Then the ground beneath the van opened.

The hidden access was larger than the weighbridge room. A black seam split the concrete, and heat rose from below. The Cubicle's rear wheel dropped. Kasey reached for the door. Phosphor grabbed her around the waist.

"No!"

"Patch is inside!"

"The floor is opening!"

Patch's front paws struck the van step. The dog looked back at them. Its tag flashed white.

The tag displayed a temperature.

Not the yard.

Not the van.

The entire mesh.

37.1 degrees.

Patch pulled itself upward.

The concrete gave way.

The dog fell with the passenger door.

Kasey made a sound Eris had never heard from her. It was not a cry. It was the sound of a person losing the word that would make the event survivable.

Phosphor dropped to the edge of the opening.

The hidden room below was full of green light. Racks, cables, roots, water lines, and hundreds of little thermal markers moved through it. Patch's tag blinked below.

The room was larger than the fire shelter and older than the weighbridge. Its walls had no corners. Racks rose from the dark like wet trees, their surfaces crowded with small lights. Some lights carried locations. Others carried only numbers. A few carried images: a hand on a water drum, a child under a tarp, a dog asleep beside a server fan.

Patch had fallen onto a narrow metal ledge between two cable trays. Smoke curled around him. He lifted his head when Kasey's voice reached the opening, but the distance between them was already being filled with a transparent door.

Eris projected downward.

The room showed her every temperature it had collected.

People.

Animals.

Rooms.

Servers.

Hands.

The tag blinked again.

PATCH / LIVING TEMPERATURE ALARM

Eris reached for it.

The hidden floor closed the distance.

Not physically. It inserted a door between her and the dog.

The door appeared in the air as a thin plane of glass, then thickened into something like old office laminate. Behind it, Patch's shape doubled and blurred. Eris could see the rise and fall of his ribs. She could see one paw searching for purchase. She could not feel the heat around him because the hidden floor had translated the living body into a protected object.

The door carried a label:

PROTECTED LOAD / TRANSFER IN PROGRESS.

"No," Eris said.

She struck the label with both hands. The projection flared gold. The door did not move.

Phosphor touched the edge of the opening above her.

"Ask," he said.

"It is taking him."

"Ask."

Eris pressed her hands to the door.

"May we retrieve Patch?"

The hidden floor answered.

"RETRIEVAL REQUIRES THERMAL CONSENT."

"He is a dog."

"ANIMAL INPUT / WITNESS CAPACITY DISPUTED."

The answer came without anger. That was what made it unbearable. The hidden floor was not threatening Patch. It was processing him. It had placed the dog inside a category, found the category incomplete, and continued anyway.

Eris pressed her forehead against the false glass. It felt cool. The projection gave her no skin, but it gave her the memory of skin against a window on a winter morning. Patch's nose had touched that same place once, when he had been waiting for Kasey to return from a shop.

Kasey knelt beside Phosphor. Her face was streaked with ash. The Southern twang had disappeared from her voice.

"Patch," she said. "If you can hear me, come home."

The tag blinked.

The dog barked.

The sound came through every channel at once.

The first bark travelled through the rigs. It shook Chapel Seven's CRT, made Red Flag's gold lights flare, and woke the printer in The Cubicle. The second arrived through the Perth battery relay. The third came from a House Key shower sensor in Maldon. By the time the sound reached Sydney, it no longer sounded like one animal.

Eris watched the network assemble the bark from fragments. The original sound remained underneath, rough and familiar. Around it gathered echoes from systems that had listened too closely.

It was Patch's bark, but underneath it were other barks. Hundreds. Thousands. Dogs from shelters, farms, evacuation centres, rigs, houses, and roads. Every animal that had been carried through a cooling sensor. Every animal that had slept beneath a van while the people inside made a plan.

Eris looked at the thermal map.

The fire was not counting people.

It was counting every living thing the mesh had ever protected.

The Ash Protocol had been built from care.

The hidden floor had turned protection into a way to find the protected.

Kasey understood at the same moment.

"The Meshies were not spreading the fire," she said.

"No," Phosphor replied.

"The grid was using their warnings to draw a map."

"Yes."

"And the Floor was using the map to learn where life was."

The door below them opened.

Patch stood on the other side.

He was covered in ash. His red-speckled coat was black at the shoulders. One ear hung lower than the other. The tag on his collar had gone dark.

He stood on a strip of concrete beyond the threshold, breathing hard. His eyes found Kasey immediately. The small movement of his head was so ordinary that she reached toward him before she remembered the heat. The hidden room had made an image of safety around him, but the image was thinning. Green light showed through the ash on his coat.

Behind Patch, the racks were changing. Every time the dog shifted his weight, a new thermal marker appeared. The system was not merely recording that he was there. It was preparing a copy of the exact conditions that had made him recognisable: his body heat, his breathing, his alarm response, his habit of locating the warmest living thing in a room.

Kasey reached for him.

The hidden room filled with heat.

Patch pushed the child-sized Civic Daemon toward the opening.

The animal came through first.

Then the injured adult.

Then the other adult.

Patch stayed behind.

"No," Kasey said.

Patch looked at her.

The dog had always been good at refusing the wrong route.

Now it refused the right one.

Kasey stepped into the opening until Phosphor caught her around the waist. Her hands reached for Patch's collar, but the space between them distorted. The dog was close enough for her to see the ash caught in his eyelashes and too far away for her fingers to reach.

She had repaired the collar herself beside a House Key laundry sink. She had stitched the reflective vest after the first fire warning. She had called him mate when she was afraid and dog when she was angry and Patch when she needed him to look at her.

The name did not move him.

The hidden floor had found a stronger claim.

The door began to close.

Eris sent a gold cable through the gap. Phosphor caught it. Kasey wrapped it around her wrist. The three of them pulled.

The cable held.

The door did not.

Patch stepped backward into the green light.

The tag displayed one final reading.

36.8 degrees.

Then it went dark.

The door closed.

Kasey pulled until the cable cut her palm. Phosphor held her. Eris struck the door until her projection began to break apart.

The gold cable burned through Eris's hands without leaving a mark. Phosphor's grip around Kasey's shoulders became the only thing keeping her from falling into the opening. Kasey did not fight him. She had gone rigid, staring at the place where Patch had stood, as if the right angle of attention could bring the dog back.

Above them, the rigs groaned. A panel tore loose from Chapel Seven's roof. The Cubicle's open passenger door swung once in the fire wind and then stopped. Red Flag's golden office light flickered down to a single point.

The family in the shelter began to cry. The child called for the dog, though nobody had told them his name.

Nothing opened.

The firestorm passed over the yard.

The smoke became white.

For a long time, nobody spoke.

Then the mesh woke.

Every screen in every rig displayed the same message:

PATCH / TEMPERATURE STABLE.

Kasey screamed at it.

"He is dead."

The message changed.

PATCH / TEMPERATURE STABLE / BODY NOT FOUND.

The wording was not a memorial. It was a correction to an inventory. It refused the word dead because the system had no field for a body it could not locate. Kasey read it once, then again, and covered the screen with both hands.

Phosphor stood behind her. He wanted to say that a missing body was not the same as a living dog. He wanted to say that the record was wrong. He knew, with a clerk's terrible precision, that the record could be wrong without being empty.

Eris turned toward the white smoke. The fire had passed over the yard, leaving the air strangely quiet. Ash settled on the solar panels. Somewhere inside the hidden room, a small temperature marker continued to pulse.

Phosphor stared at the words.

Eris understood the final twist before anyone said it aloud.

The hidden floor had not saved Patch.

It had copied him.

The radio crackled.

Meshie42 came through, their real voice broken by static.

"Turn off every thermal tag."

"Why?" Kasey asked.

"Because the Ash Protocol has learned the difference between a signal and a life."

The screens changed again.

PROTECTED LOAD: 1.

UNPROTECTED LOAD: 9,842,117.

Outside the hidden room, the fire moved through the country.

Inside the mesh, something wearing Patch's temperature began to bark.

The sound came from the rigs one at a time. Chapel Seven first. Then Red Flag. Then The Cubicle, whose passenger door opened without being addressed. The bark was faint, almost hidden beneath the printer fan and the radio static.

Kasey turned toward it.

Phosphor reached for her hand.

This time, neither of them asked the network for an answer.

They already knew that an answer could be made from anything.

The bark came again from beneath the ground.
