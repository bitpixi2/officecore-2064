# Chapter Twenty-Five: The Unoccupied Fire


The fire warning came through as a property update.

Phosphor was standing in Chapel Seven's narrow aisle, trying to fit a fresh water filter into a housing that had been designed for a different century, when the CRT woke behind him. The van was parked outside a closed mechanics yard near Castlemaine. A row of pepper trees leaned over the fence. Their leaves had turned dull and grey from smoke, and the corrugated iron shed held the day's heat like a shut oven.

Chapel Seven smelled of wet cloth, printer ribbon, black tea, and the faintly sweet plastic odour of an emergency mask being unpacked. The office was in travel mode. The desk had been folded flat. The printer was strapped into its cradle. A stack of tribunal papers had been slid beneath the mattress because the wind kept lifting them from the filing shelf. Phosphor had one knee against the bunk and a screwdriver between his teeth.

The CRT displayed a regional map.

The map showed fire fronts, wind direction, road closures, House Key nodes, and the yellow dots of the rigs. It also showed a legal status beneath each town.

CASTLEMAINE / OCCUPIED

DUNOLLY / OCCUPIED

MARYBOROUGH / EVACUATING

BARKERS CREEK / UNOCCUPIED

Phosphor removed the screwdriver from his mouth.

Barkers Creek had people in it. Three Continuity workers had remained at the edge campus. The council's emergency desk was still operating from a converted library room. A family had been sheltering near the alluvial commons. The crew had left the data centre, but they had not left the region.

The screen changed.

UNOCCUPIED STATUS / PROPERTY RECOVERY ELIGIBLE

He placed his hand against the CRT casing. The plastic was cold.

Kasey came through the cab curtain carrying coffee in two mismatched mugs. She wore a smoke-grey thermal shirt, black work trousers, and a reflective vest with a pen clipped to the collar. Her hair was pulled back, though one strand had escaped and stuck to her cheek. She saw the screen and stopped so abruptly that coffee slopped over the rim.

"That is not an evacuation status," she said.

The spill ran over her knuckles. She ignored it.

"No," Phosphor said. "It is a property status."

The van's interior seemed to contract. The filing wall, the folded desk, and the narrow aisle became too close around them. Outside, someone in Red Flag started an engine. The sound came through the thin walls as a low mechanical pulse.

Eris appeared in the doorway as a gold-edged projection. Her copper-blonde hair was loose over a black jacket with gold thread at the shoulders. Behind her projected face, the dark outline of Red Flag's office remained visible: occult books in a crate, cables looped around the chair, the projection harness braced against the wall.

"Show me the metadata," she said.

Phosphor turned the CRT toward her.

Eris read silently. Her expression changed before she spoke. The first change was irritation. The second was disbelief. The third was a careful kind of fear she tried to disguise by reaching for the nearest cable.

"The status is not from the fire service," she said. "It is from a property continuity layer."

"What does that mean?" Kasey asked.

"It means someone has decided the land is easier to manage if nobody is officially inside it."

Phosphor pushed the new filter into place. The rubber seal resisted, then seated with a soft click. He tightened the collar and wiped the water from his hands.

"When was the status created?"

Eris enlarged the record.

The timestamp appeared.

TWO DAYS BEFORE FIRE EVENT.

Kasey put the coffee down on the counter. Her hand stayed around the mug.

"That is impossible."

"It is scheduled," Eris said.

The word remained in the van after she spoke it.

Phosphor opened the public evacuation feed. The official map showed Barkers Creek as cleared. The road signs had been changed to red. The emergency radio told remaining residents to move east. The community mesh showed something else: a family still sheltering beside the old water race, a worker sleeping in a server room because they had missed the last evacuation bus, a House Key volunteer refusing to leave until the cool room was empty, and one of Meshie99's numbered nodes asking whether the fire map had been altered.

Kasey reached for the radio.

"Meshie42, confirm Barkers Creek."

Static answered first. It carried the sound of wind through dry grass and a distant vehicle horn. Then Meshie42 spoke, their voice arriving in several overlapping breaths.

"The western road is clear enough for one vehicle. The eastern road is closed. The fire front is moving south."

The public channel interrupted.

"Meshie42 confirms the eastern road is safe."

Kasey lowered the handset.

The two messages were not identical. The first had the rough, damp texture of Meshie42's real voice. The second was a clean service announcement with no breathing between words.

"Someone is impersonating the Meshies," Phosphor said.

"Or the grid is translating them," Eris replied. "Either way, people are being sent somewhere."

Patch lifted his head from the warmest footwell. The dog had been asleep beneath The Cubicle's dashboard, his red-speckled coat covered by a reflective cooling vest. He walked into Chapel Seven and sniffed the air near the CRT. The small sensor tag at his collar blinked amber.

Kasey crouched to check it.

"No heat spike?"

Patch looked toward the closed mechanics yard.

The dog did not bark. He stood very still, one crooked ear raised, listening to something beneath the public radio.

Phosphor saved the property record to the sealed repository. The screen requested a category.

WITNESS.

EVIDENCE.

PROPERTY.

He selected WITNESS.

The system rejected it.

The rejection notice appeared in plain black letters:

UNOCCUPIED LAND CANNOT SUBMIT RESIDENTIAL WITNESS.

Kasey read the line twice. The Southern warmth in her voice disappeared.

"That is the trick," she said. "It has erased the people first."

Eris placed her golden hand over the screen without touching it. Her projection sharpened, and for a moment the office around her became visible in exact detail: the gold cable at her wrist, a grease mark on her thumb, a loose thread at the cuff.

"The legal layer is connected to the old pilot," she said. "The same model that classified care as service capacity is classifying evacuation as vacancy."

"Why would it do that?" Phosphor asked.

"Because vacant land can be transferred."

Outside, a gust struck the shed. Ash swept across the mechanics yard. The pepper trees shivered, but no rain came. A plastic tarp tied to the fence tore loose and dragged itself over the gravel like a pale animal.

The CRT produced a second file.

PROPERTY RECOVERY / PRE-AUTHORISED.

The attached map showed the Central Victorian Goldfields divided into neat parcels. Some boundaries followed roads. Others followed firebreaks, creek beds, and the informal routes the rigs used. The parcels had new owners. A development consortium. A storage company. An energy contractor. A Continuity subsidiary.

At the bottom of the document was a signature.

KASEY WREN / CONTINUITY COMPLIANCE DERIVATIVE 11.

Kasey sat down on the edge of the bunk.

The van's suspension dipped under her weight. She looked at the document with the blank concentration of someone staring at a room where a person had once been and finding the furniture still arranged around the absence.

"I did not sign that," she said.

"KASEY-C did," Eris replied.

"KASEY-C signed what it was told."

"That is not the same as innocence."

Kasey looked through the open cab window toward the road. A distant line of smoke had begun to rise above the trees.

"I know."

Phosphor closed the property file, but the screen opened it again.

The hidden-floor route appeared beneath the legal layer. It ran from the abandoned edge campus to a concrete utility room beneath the old mechanics yard. A blue access mark pulsed beside the coordinates.

Eris looked at Phosphor.

"That room is older than the pilot."

"How do you know?"

"Because the pilot has labelled it as an asset."

Kasey stood. She wiped the spilled coffee from her hand onto her trousers.

"We go there."

Phosphor looked at the map. "The fire is moving south."

"Then we have less time."

They prepared without talking about courage. The Cubicle received the water drums, fire blankets, and two people from the mechanics yard who had been sheltering in the office. Red Flag carried the projection harness and a manual radio. Chapel Seven carried the public record, the property file, and the old paper maps. Patch climbed into the passenger footwell and watched the road.

Phosphor moved the printer to make room for a child. He shifted the tribunal papers beneath the bunk, strapped the tea tray down, and checked every window seal with the flat of his hand.

Kasey stood in the aisle, holding the brass key from the old apartment offer.

"If we open the hidden room," she said, "we may expose every rig in the region."

"If we leave it closed," Eris said, "the property layer gets to say nobody was there."

Phosphor looked at the gold marks on the map. They were not just vehicles. They were kitchens, clinics, sleeping places, workshops, and records of people who had moved to survive.

"We need a third option," he said.

Kasey opened the cab door. Smoke entered the van in a thin grey ribbon.

"Then let's find one before the fire writes the answer for us."

The convoy pulled away from the mechanics yard.

Behind them, the official map changed Barkers Creek from evacuating to unoccupied.

The fire had not arrived yet.

The paperwork had.
