# Chapter Seven: Convoy Minutes

*Close third person through Phosphor.*

They reached the Newstead House Key after dark, in a convoy that looked less like an emergency service than a collection of neglected appliances refusing to die.

The neighbourhood house sat behind a row of pepper trees and a weatherboard hall with a faded mural of hands holding a sun. Its lights were low to conserve power. A handwritten sign had been taped to the gate:

`SHOWERS / LAUNDRY / COOL ROOM / TWO SOCKETS / NO FIXED ADDRESS REQUIRED`

The last line had been underlined three times.

Victoria had inherited House Key from an earlier political imagination, a network of neighbourhood houses that had once been expected to provide adult learning, tea, safe rooms, printers, and people who knew where the spare blankets were. In 2064, the network had become one of the few pieces of civic infrastructure that still understood the difference between a house and a home.

Perth had the heat cooperatives. Sydney had the Porchlight Grid, where people with fixed homes elected to become safe hosts in exchange for computer repairs, printer help, and the occasional emergency garden bed. Victoria had House Key, which had already decided, decades earlier, that a person did not need to own the walls around them to be allowed to wash in a building.

The shower coordinator met them with a clipboard and a torch.

"You get seven minutes each," she said. "Water pressure is terrible. The hot water system is solar-assisted and emotionally complicated. Laundry is one load per rig. Cool room is for food, medication, and anyone who has stopped making sense from heat."

Patch sat beside Phosphor and looked up at her.

"The dog gets priority if the dog is overheating," she added.

Patch's tail tapped once against the gravel.

"The rat?" Phosphor asked.

Semaphore emerged from GhostAgent's cable pouch as if summoned by an administrative loophole.

The coordinator leaned closer. Semaphore stared back with bright black eyes.

"The rat goes in the cool room if the owner signs the cleaning agreement."

`GHOSTAGENT: Semaphore is not an owner. Semaphore is a cohabiting witness.`

"Then the cohabiting witness signs," she said.

Semaphore stole the corner of the cleaning form.

"Good enough," the coordinator said.

The hall smelled of wet towels, disinfectant, eucalyptus, and the sweet, exhausted odour of people who had been outside too long. On the wall, a community noticeboard held flyers for a repair cafe, an Auslan gardening circle, a tax clinic, a grief group, and a class called `INTRODUCTION TO SAFE PASSWORDS FOR PEOPLE WHO HAVE BEEN LIED TO BY THEIR APPLIANCES`.

Phosphor stood in the doorway of Chapel Seven and watched the convoy unpack itself.

The rigs had been designed as offices first and homes second. Every movement required a negotiation with another movement. A desk became a bed. A bed became a storage shelf. The tea tray clipped under a crash rail. The printer sat on a foam cradle above the water filter. Three blankets came out of the same drawer as a box of network terminators. Eris had to move her occult desk manuals from Red Flag's office shelves into a waterproof laundry basket before the van could make room for Patch.

"You are putting the books in with the towels," Phosphor observed.

"They are both forms of protection," Eris said. Her copper-blonde hair was loose now, darkened at the ends by smoke and gold where the House Key lights caught it. She wore a black mesh overshirt over a pale thermal singlet, with reflective thread stitched along the sleeves in patterns that resembled tiny circuit diagrams.

"The books are not absorbent."

"You have not read the occult ones."

Phosphor decided this was a statement he would not authenticate.

The Cubicle's water pump had failed while they were turning into the car park. It had leaked into the cable drawer, making the network connectors shine like small drowned insects. Kasey sat on the gravel with her knees tucked beneath her, dismantling the drawer with a magnetic driver and the absolute expression of someone trying to negotiate with an object that had already chosen disobedience.

"How bad?" Phosphor asked.

"The pump is offended."

"That is not a measurement."

"It is the measurement available to me."

She held up a wet cable.

"We can still get water through the external tap. The tank sensor is lying. The pump is making a clicking sound that implies it has started a private religion."

"Office-dharma does not approve of private religions in infrastructure."

"Office-dharma is welcome to replace the pump."

Phosphor turned to Chapel Seven, where the mail server fan had started whining. Semaphore had climbed onto the warm casing and was using the fan's vibration to scratch its back.

"Semaphore," he said.

`GHOSTAGENT: Semaphore declines technical responsibility.`

The food-rescue kitchen served dinner from folding tables under the hall awning. There was pumpkin soup, rice, flatbread, bruised pears cut into careful slices, and a tray of small pastries that had been rescued from a bakery's failed delivery. The soup tasted of cumin, black pepper, stock, and the faint sweetness of pumpkin that had been stored too close to onions. Steam dampened Phosphor's face. He could feel the cold ash on his jacket soften.

Nobody asked the crew to volunteer in exchange for food. People sorted vegetables because the vegetables needed sorting. They carried boxes because boxes needed carrying. The distinction mattered. Care offered as a debt had a different temperature from care offered as a shared fact.

Kasey stood at the refuse station with three battered tubs labelled `REUSE`, `HAZARD`, and `BIN`. She removed the lid from a bag of food scraps and stared into it as if a policy violation had been hiding under the banana peels.

Phosphor came to stand beside her.

"You are doing the face again," he said.

"What face?"

"The face of someone who believes a receipt has betrayed them."

Kasey tied off the bag.

"Trash is proof of life."

She said it lightly at first, then looked at the black shapes of the rigs beyond the hall. Their solar awnings made a ragged canopy against the night. Blue Skystitch locks winked on the roofs. A pressure fan came alive inside Red Flag and pushed a ribbon of filtered air through the open side hatch. Somewhere in The Cubicle, a damp cable was being dried with a hairdryer borrowed from the House Key bathroom.

"A receipt says somebody bought something," Kasey continued. "Trash says what happened after. It tells you whether people had water, whether food arrived, whether packaging was reusable, whether medicine was used or left untouched. It tells you where the sanitation system stopped working. If all of us collectively stop caring, the bins fill, pests arrive, disease rises, and people start blaming each other for the smell. Then they stop trusting the kitchen, the council, the neighbours, the whole idea that a place can keep them alive."

Phosphor looked into the refuse tubs. A cracked charger. Two citrus peels. A strip of thermal adhesive. A plastic spoon with pumpkin on it. A torn label from a water filter.

"Every system leaves a receipt," he said.

Kasey glanced at him.

He found himself speaking before he had sorted the thought.

"On one expedition, I saw a courtyard kept habitable by windcatchers that were maintained by three generations of people who never described the work as architecture. In another place, a wetland cooled the air before it reached a school. In another, a fire laboratory measured heat by looking at what failed first: seals, joints, paint, people. Warmth and cooling are never one heroic machine. They are a chain of small permissions. Shade. Water. Air movement. Insulation. A person who remembers to clean the filter."

"You are talking about energy now," Kasey said.

"I am talking about the politics of not being cooked."

Eris appeared behind them, carrying two mugs of coffee.

"Magic?" she asked.

"House Key coffee," Kasey said.

"Then it is an act of courage."

The blend was Melbourne Magic, dark and fragrant, with enough milk to make the bitterness feel deliberate. Phosphor drank and tasted roasted nuts, wet pavement, and a small burnt edge that reminded him of the morning's fire.

Eris pointed at the rigs.

"At least our insulation is better than a lot of the two-million-dollar Australian houses."

Kasey nodded toward the hall.

"Our insulation is also portable."

"We have a thermal curtain where a wall should be," Eris said.

"That is called adaptive architecture."

"That is called a bedsheet with a grant."

The laughter came out of them unevenly. It did not erase the smoke, the gate, or the black ceramic key hidden in Kasey's pocket. It made a small room around those things.

Later, after the final shower slot, the House Key hall changed shape. Folding tables went against the walls. Chairs stacked in the corner. A class notice was moved from the floor because a person sleeping beneath it had started to read the same sentence over and over. The rigs became bedrooms by subtraction.

Phosphor secured the tea tray, latched the printer ribbon, clipped the water gauge into its travel position, and made a line in the maintenance ledger. The line read:

`ONE PUMP OFFENDED / ONE COMMUNITY HOUSE RECEIVED / ONE DOG COOL / ONE RAT UNACCOUNTED FOR`

He set the pen down.

The CRT in Chapel Seven woke.

On the screen, two messages arrived at once.

`PERTH NODE: BATTERY ROUTING ADVICE ATTACHED. OFFICIAL GRID HAS CALLED PEER-SHARED POWER AN UNLICENSED UTILITY.`

`SYDNEY NODE: PORCHLIGHT HOST ACCEPTED THE VEHICLE. SYSTEM REJECTED THE PEOPLE SLEEPING INSIDE IT.`

Phosphor filed both under:

`SAME SYSTEM / DIFFERENT WEATHER`

Then a third message appeared, from Meshie99.

`COOLING IS NOT A LOCATION. DO NOT LET THEM ASK WHERE WE ARE. ASK WHAT WE ARE KEEPING ALIVE.`

Under it, a fourth line appeared in a different font.

`CUSTOMER CARE CENTRE ROUTE CONFIRMED.`

Patch began barking at the hall wall.

Semaphore, who had returned with a red hazard label and half a biscuit, climbed into the cable cradle and went still.

Phosphor looked toward the dark windows. Beyond them, the pepper trees moved in the hot night wind. The old neighbourhood house held its doors. The rigs held their heat. Somewhere under a building that had been designed to process complaints, an unseen floor had accepted their route.

The next morning was already waiting in the walls.
