# Central Victorian Goldfields

## Status

Bitpixi-led setting research and fiction guidance. This uses real regional Victorian town names as geographic anchors. The 2064 institutions, alluvial commons, satellite network, and plot events are fictional.

## Story Corridor

Book One moves through or takes cues from the central Goldfields corridor:

- `Clunes`: an early site of the 1851 Victorian discoveries; useful for old civic rooms, annual rituals, bookish visitors, and a town that knows how to host temporary populations.
- `Ballarat`: a major city-scale Goldfields anchor with big public architecture, worker history, and the pressure of tourism packaging difficult history.
- `Castlemaine` and `Chewton`: former Forest Creek / Mount Alexander diggings territory, dense with routes, older shopfronts, ridgelines, archive material, and contested stories of the rush.
- `Maldon` and `Newstead`: smaller-town texture, heritage streets, roadside logistics, old rail and water infrastructure, useful places for a convoy to sleep, trade, or be conspicuously noticed.
- `Daylesford` and Hepburn: water, health, retreat culture, and former alluvial/deep-lead landscapes; good for a too-expensive future wellness facility beside a very old extractive scar.
- `Maryborough`, `Dunolly`, `Talbot`, `Tarnagulla`, and the Golden Triangle: panning, metal-detecting culture, small museums, old tailings, road detours, and community knowledge of a landscape repeatedly dug up and repurposed.
- `Heathcote` and `Bendigo`: larger regional links for warehouses, old mining machinery, data relay manufacture, archives, transport, and civic paperwork with more consequences.

The novel should not treat this corridor as an empty retro set. It crosses lived-in Country with its own present communities, environmental pressures, and cultural memory. The Castlemaine, Maldon, Daylesford, Dunolly, and Bendigo section is on Dja Dja Wurrung Country. Ballarat includes Wadawurrung and Dja Dja Wurrung Country, so a scene set there needs town-level accuracy rather than a blanket label.

## Historical Grounding

Gold discoveries in 1851 at Clunes, Buninyong, Castlemaine / Forest Creek, and Bendigo transformed Victoria. The rush brought global migration, including a substantial Chinese mining history, alongside discriminatory violence and major environmental disruption. Forests were cleared, waterways were damaged, and Traditional Owners were further marginalised by the influx and extraction.

For the central route, name Dja Dja Wurrung Country where appropriate. Do not invent sacred sites, fictionalise cultural authority, or make a character's access to land a substitute for actual consent. A future draft using detailed Djarra history, language, or cultural practice should involve specific research and permission.

## 2064 Goldfields Use

In Officecore 2064, the goldfields are a palimpsest of extraction systems:

- old water races become cooling trenches, walking routes, and illegal fibre paths
- abandoned poppet heads hold relay gear, weather sensors, and protest banners
- heritage shopfronts become tiny departments, repair labs, laundries, and slow cafes with very fast networks
- former tailings flats become monitored alluvial commons, solar fields, caravan stops, and contested flood buffers
- mine records, title maps, and licence registers become clues in the Unallocated Floor

The point is not that the old gold rush was charming. The point is that new infrastructure keeps inheriting its routes, promises, exclusions, and paperwork.

## VIC Miner's Right In The Story

Current Victorian law treats a Miner's Right as an individual permit for recreational prospecting with non-mechanical hand tools. It does not provide open access to every place or permit environmental or cultural harm.

In 2064, the crew keeps that basic civic idea. Each adult carries a current `VIC Miner's Right` in their lanyard wallet. A digital check shows the holder, the permitted activity, the site host, rehabilitation requirements, and any current fire or access restrictions.

For a panning scene, use these story rules:

- the crew has an individual right and the site's explicit authorisation
- they use pans, sieves, and hand tools only
- they pause immediately for fire, flood, heritage, habitat, or local access restrictions
- they backfill and restore the site after any digging
- another person at the site can challenge their access or their account of what they found
- the gold is never the only value in the scene; water, witness, memory, repair, and local trust matter too

## The Panning Day

The Book One panning sequence happens at the fictional `Dunolly Alluvial Commons`, a community-managed, restoration-monitored site outside Dunolly. It is a waystation, not a treasure cave.

The crew stops because their Southern Skystitch terminal has heat-damaged its gold contact mesh. They need both a repair and a pause. While licensed locals show them where hand panning is allowed, Phosphor recovers a tiny gold-backed archive wafer from newly shifted tailings. It contains a Continuity records index that points toward the dead call centre.

The scene should do five things:

1. Let the crew enjoy being outside and temporarily unhurried.
2. Give Phosphor a physical relationship with trace evidence rather than another screen.
3. Let Kasey's need for control collide with a process that requires patience.
4. Give Eris a chance to be unexpectedly competent at careful, low-tech work.
5. Make the discovery create an obligation to the commons and the people who host it.

## Southern Skystitch

Southern Skystitch is the fictional 2064 answer to consumer satellite internet: a low-Earth-orbit mesh made in Australia and maintained through a mix of public contracts, regional cooperatives, and rig-town repair culture.

- Full name: `Southern Skystitch Mesh`
- Common terminal name: `stitch panel`
- Build story: rugged terminal housings and field-repair kits are assembled in retooled workshops around Bendigo; orbital hardware and governance are distributed nationally
- What it does: carries emergency comms, local caches, clinic data, maps, event feeds, and ordinary embarrassing messages between places with weak terrestrial coverage
- What can fail: smoke, ash, heat, weather, line of sight, power draw, orbital congestion, data caps, political throttling, damaged contact mesh, or a cranky local relay cooperative
- What makes it Officecore: every terminal has a serial number, maintenance ledger, access tier, unpaid invoice history, and an argument attached to it

In Chapter One, Chapel Seven's stitch panel keeps Rooby and the Hackeroos network online long enough for the receipt map to become a shared public resource.
