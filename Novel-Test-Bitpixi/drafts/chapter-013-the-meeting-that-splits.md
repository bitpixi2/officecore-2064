# Chapter Thirteen: The Meeting That Splits

*Close third person through Phosphor.*

The vote took place behind a Bunnings loading dock because the Bunnings had closed for the evening, the loading dock had shade, and somebody had found a power outlet that believed in generosity.

The building's orange facade glowed against the smoke. The old slogan above the service entrance had lost several letters, so it now appeared to promise:

`EVERYTHING FOR EVERYONE EXCEPT...`

The missing words had been covered by a solar tarp.

Rigs formed a crooked semicircle around the meeting table. The table was a sheet of recycled shelving balanced on milk crates. A tray of sausage rolls sat at its centre beneath a handwritten sign:

`CATERING / NOT GOVERNMENT / PLEASE DO NOT ELECT`

Patch slept under the table with its reflective vest folded beneath its chin. Semaphore patrolled the minutes from inside the cable crate, dragging red labels into a private nest. GhostAgent's cursor moved across three projected screens at once, assigning timestamps to interruptions.

Phosphor stood at the front with his blank sheet ready.

He could smell sawdust, hot plastic, onions from the food van across the road, and the faint medicinal tang of smoke caught in everybody's clothes. The loading dock concrete held the day's heat. When people shifted their feet, the soles of their boots made small adhesive sounds.

Kasey stood beside the table, shoulders square, black jacket powdered with ash. The cold-key had left a pale mark around her wrist. She had not surrendered it. She had not used it. It remained in the pocket of her trousers like a private weather system.

Eris leaned against Red Flag's golden-lit side hatch. Her copper-blonde hair had been braided with a cable tie. She wore a gold-threaded jacket over a shirt from a failed civic-game launch, the front printed with a smiling cartoon ballot box that had been crossed out in black marker.

"We need an agenda," Kasey said.

"We need a decision," said the person from the snack table.

"The decision requires an agenda."

"The snack table has an agenda. It is to remain uneaten until the minutes are approved."

Phosphor looked at the tray.

"The snack table is not a participant."

The tray moved.

Nobody touched it.

Semaphore emerged from the cable crate, climbed onto the milk crate, and sat beside the sausage rolls.

`GHOSTAGENT: Semaphore objects to exclusion on the grounds that food is an infrastructure category.`

Kasey closed her eyes briefly.

"Fine. Catering has observer status."

The tray stopped moving.

Phosphor wrote it down.

`CATERING / OBSERVER STATUS / NO VOTING RIGHTS / UNRESOLVED`

The first speaker wanted the pilot.

They were a person with a shaved head, a patched silver coat, and an air filter clipped to their collar. They spoke in a soft voice that made every sentence sound like an apology to the future.

"The pilot would fund cooling," they said. "It would pay for legal berths, battery maintenance, insurance, and a fixed emergency contact. We can tell ourselves that independence is beautiful, but beauty does not replace a compressor."

The second speaker wanted to destroy it.

They wore a high-vis vest over a silk dress and carried a crowbar with a pink handle. Their voice had the clipped upward twang of somebody who had spent years arguing with call centres and had begun to use their own script.

"The pilot takes the work we already do and turns it into something we must subscribe to," they said. "It learns who gives water, who shares batteries, who can be guilted into staying late. Then it calls the prediction a service. That is not funding. That is a landlord with a database."

The third speaker had no position yet.

They had brought a folding chair, a pressure cooker, and a printed copy of Continuity's offer. Their hands smelled of coriander and engine oil.

"If we refuse all fixed systems," they said, "we become dependent on whoever can repair a van. If we accept the pilot, we become dependent on the company. I would like a third dependency, preferably one with a shorter queue."

"That is not how governance works," Kasey said.

"It is how lunch works."

The Perth battery cooperative joined by delayed audio. Its connection arrived in pieces, a voice from a white-hot yard where reflective curtains moved between storage units.

`PERTH NODE: OUR COOPERATIVE WILL LOAN POWER TO VICTORIAN RIGS IF THE LOAN CANNOT BE CONVERTED INTO A CONTINUITY SCORE.`

The Sydney Porchlight Grid joined next. Their audio carried the rumble of a tunnel relay and the distant sound of a harbour ferry.

`SYDNEY NODE: WE CAN OFFER TWO HOST HOMES, ONE PRINTER, AND A SHOWER APPOINTMENT IN NEWTOWN. THE HOUSES ARE FIXED. THE PEOPLE STILL ARE NOT.`

Kasey looked down at the shelving-table.

"The pilot would give us money," she said. "It would also give the company a way to decide which of us is worth saving."

"You wrote the language," Eris said.

The loading dock changed temperature.

Phosphor saw Kasey's hand tighten around the cold-key through the fabric of her pocket.

"Yes," Kasey said.

The admission moved through the gathered people faster than any official notice. It did not make her a villain. It made her available to blame.

"I wrote fixed accountability because Continuity was losing people in the gaps between agencies," Kasey continued. "I believed a person should be able to prove who was responsible when a service failed. I did not understand that the company would turn the proof into a market for the person themselves."

"You should have understood," said the crowbar.

"Yes."

The simple agreement silenced the dock.

Phosphor watched Eris look at Kasey. Their old intimacy was not romantic. It was the intimacy of two people who had once built a dangerous thing together and still remembered the exact sound it made when it worked.

The stranger in the Continuity raincoat stepped forward.

Nobody had seen them arrive. They carried a stack of pre-filled ballots and a lanyard with no name, only a QR code.

"Continuity is not asking you to become owned," the stranger said. "The pilot simply creates a shared service layer."

"You have brought ballots," Kasey said.

"For convenience."

"The vote has not been called."

"The outcome is already urgent."

Phosphor reached for the ballots.

The stranger released them.

Each ballot had one question:

`DO YOU ACCEPT THE CARE CAPACITY OFFER?`

The answer boxes were:

`YES`

`YES, WITH CONDITIONS`

`YES, DEFERRED`

There was no `NO`.

Semaphore climbed up the stranger's coat, reached the top pocket, and pulled out a fourth stack.

The rat dragged it across the concrete.

On the cover was a red stamp:

`NO / RECORD REQUIRED`

Phosphor picked it up.

The stranger's face changed.

"That material is proprietary."

"It is now evidence," Phosphor said.

He stamped the top page.

`RECEIVED UNDER PROTEST / WITNESS PRESENT`

The loading dock lights flickered.

Rooby's event feed opened on the nearest screen. Messages arrived from regional House Key nodes, Perth battery yards, Sydney apartment hosts, roadside mechanics, food banks, and a group in Ballarat who had not yet decided whether they were a rig town or just a parking problem with good Wi-Fi.

The messages disagreed.

Some asked for access to the pilot. Some demanded it be destroyed. Some wanted the source code. Some wanted the servers to stay online because their medication fridge depended on them. One message simply read:

`WE HAVE A DOG AND CANNOT BE EVICTED DURING SUMMER.`

Patch lifted its head.

Phosphor wrote the sentence down.

The vote began.

It failed immediately.

The first problem was that nobody agreed who was present. The second was that several people were present through devices. The third was that a person in Perth had been counted twice by the Sydney relay and not at all by the House Key node. The fourth was that the snack table had observer status but the sausage rolls had been marked as a quorum by GhostAgent's parser.

The fifth problem was the Floor.

When Phosphor opened the governance repository, a new file appeared in the branch:

`MINUTES / MEETING THAT SPLITS / AUTHOR UNKNOWN`

He did not create it.

The file contained a single line:

`A DEPARTMENT MAY BE REAL BEFORE IT IS LEGAL.`

Then another:

`A DEPARTMENT MAY BE LEGAL BEFORE IT IS ALIVE.`

Kasey read over his shoulder.

"Close it."

"I cannot."

"Why?"

"It is asking for an owner."

Eris moved beside them.

"Give it one."

Kasey looked at the crowd, the rigs, the dog, the rat, the snack tray, and the orange facade of a store built to sell people the tools of permanence.

"No," she said. "Not one owner."

She took the marker.

On the shelving-table, beneath the ballot stack, she wrote:

`ACCOUNTABILITY IS SHARED / LIABILITY IS NOT`

The repository accepted the line.

The vote did not pass.

It did something stranger.

It created a pending department with no authority, no budget, no registered address, and thirty-seven witnesses.

The snack table was listed as catering observer.

Patch was listed as a living temperature alarm.

Semaphore was listed as an unauthorised signatory.

Phosphor was listed as `FOUNDING WITNESS / STATUS DISPUTED`.

Kasey was listed as `AUTHOR / CORRECTING PARTY`.

Eris was listed as `ACCESS ESCALATION / DO NOT SEPARATE`.

The screen flashed.

Then a final line appeared:

`DEPARTMENT OF TEMPORARY PERMANENCE / PENDING LIFE`

The stranger in the Continuity raincoat stepped backward.

The loading dock door opened behind them.

Beyond it was not the Bunnings warehouse.

It was the beige corridor from the Unallocated Floor.

At the far end, a phone began to ring.

Phosphor closed the repository.

The snack table voted to leave.

This time, nobody objected.
