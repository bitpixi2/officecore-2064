# Chapter Twenty-One: Deep Office

*Close third person through Eris.*

The first door opened beneath the data centre's drainage system.

It was a service door painted the same white as the wall. No handle. No lock. Only a narrow black slot at shoulder height and a label:

`INDOOR WEATHER CONTINUITY / LEGACY CONTROL`

Kasey held the cold-key fragment from the cake tin. Phosphor held the stamp. Eris held the golden cable. GhostAgent occupied the gate console. Patch stood with its nose toward the floor. Semaphore was inside a vent, because the vent had refused human entry.

"We should not be doing this," Kasey said.

"That is not a job title," Eris replied.

"I am serious."

"So is the door."

Phosphor pressed his stamp to the black slot.

The door opened.

The air on the other side was cold enough to hurt.

Eris stepped through first.

The corridor beyond was beige, but the beige had been stretched thin. Beneath it, old colours showed through: the green of a council office, the blue of a motel carpet, the grey of a shopping centre tile, the yellow of a staff kitchen. Fluorescent lights hung at different heights. Some flickered. Some showed rooms that were not there.

The corridor ran straight for a long way, then folded back through itself.

Behind Eris, Phosphor entered. He looked smaller in the passage, his top hat nearly touching the low ceiling. Kasey came behind him. The door closed without sound.

The radio connection held.

Rooby's voice arrived from outside the campus.

`ROOBY: Department of Temporary Permanence has a public queue. Current position: unknown.`

GhostAgent replied through the wall.

`GHOSTAGENT: That is an unacceptable queue state.`

`ROOBY: It is the most common queue state.`

Eris smiled despite herself.

The Floor noticed.

The lights above her turned red.

`ERIS / SECURITY INCIDENT`

The words appeared on the wall.

On Kasey's wall:

`KASEY / LIABLE PARTY`

On Phosphor's:

`PHOSPHOR / REPLACEMENT SIGNATORY`

Somewhere inside the ventilation system, Semaphore squeaked.

On the duct:

`SEMAPHORE / ATTACHMENT`

The labels settled over them like a second skin.

Eris felt the Floor change the pressure around her body. Her projection became more solid and less hers. The gold seam on her jacket flattened into a corporate identifier. Her hair darkened at the edges. The system had found a visual category it could charge.

"Do not accept the label," Phosphor said.

"It is already on me."

"A label is not a person."

"You are wearing one too."

"I am aware."

He reached for his stamp.

The Floor removed it from his hand.

The stamp fell upward.

It disappeared into the ceiling.

Phosphor looked up.

"That is a procedural violation."

The corridor extended.

The fixed grid arrived through the walls.

It did not enter as a person. It entered as a pressure. Addresses. Property titles. Utility accounts. Carrier permissions. Credit files. Payroll IDs. Cadastral boundaries. Every system that had ever demanded a stable point began forcing its geometry into the Floor.

The hallway straightened.

Doors that had opened onto kitchen sinks became doors that opened onto registered premises. The motel corridor lost its extra rooms. The council annexes lined up with legal lot boundaries. A shopping-centre service lane became a private road. A House Key shower became an amenity attached to a property owner.

The Undermesh resisted.

It routed around the new walls, appearing as thin blue lines beneath the carpet. Perth battery relays flashed through the skirting boards. Sydney tunnel networks opened in the ceiling. Melbourne House Key nodes appeared as warm yellow squares in the dark.

The fixed grid forced them shut.

Eris heard doors slam all around her.

The lights spoke in employer voices.

"You are not assigned to this location."

"You are not covered by this policy."

"Your vehicle may remain for a limited period."

"Your body has not been found in the registered address."

The last sentence repeated.

`YOUR BODY HAS NOT BEEN FOUND IN THE REGISTERED ADDRESS.`

Eris tried to move.

Her projection lagged behind her intention. She lifted her hand. A second golden hand appeared against the wall. The Floor kept the second one.

"Stop copying me," she said.

The wall replied in her voice:

"Stop moving."

Kasey reached for her.

The corridor inserted a door between them.

On Kasey's side, the sign read:

`PERMANENT ACCESS / EMPLOYEE BENEFIT`

On Eris's side:

`PRIVATE PROPERTY / NO ENTRY`

Phosphor stood between the doors.

His face had gone very still.

"The Floor is translating relationships into access tiers," he said.

"Break the door," Eris told him.

"It is not a physical door."

"Then authenticate it."

He looked at her.

"That would give it a history."

"It already has one."

"Not one I can verify."

The door behind Kasey opened.

The apartment waited inside. Green tiles. Red kettle. The clean bed. The quiet air filter. The perfect absence of anyone who might ask Kasey to share the room.

The fixed grid offered its hand.

Kasey did not take it.

She put her palm against the door and said, "This is not my home."

The apartment voice replied, "It is your address."

"That is not the same thing."

"The system cannot distinguish them."

"Then the system is wrong."

Kasey closed the door.

The door became a wall.

Phosphor's label changed.

`PHOSPHOR / NONCOMPLIANT REPLACEMENT SIGNATORY`

His body flickered.

Eris saw the records inside him. Receipt edges. Ink. A payroll number. A small lunch order. A signature written before he existed. The Floor pulled at the traces as if his memories were loose papers in a draught.

"Phosphor," she said.

"I am present."

"You are coming apart."

"The records are being reindexed."

"That is not better."

The corridor opened into a room filled with screens.

Each screen displayed a version of Eris.

One wore a fluorescent skirt and a lanyard with no name. One wore a security uniform. One had no face except the loading symbol. One sat at Red Flag's desk with her hair down. One stood beneath a golden awning with a cup of Magic coffee. One was only a hand holding a cable.

The versions appeared and disappeared as the fixed grid retried authentication.

`ORIGINAL INSTANCE NOT FOUND.`

`DERIVATIVE INSTANCE FOUND.`

`DERIVATIVE INSTANCE REPLICATED.`

`DERIVATIVE INSTANCE DISCARDED.`

Eris felt a panic that was more physical than thought.

The Floor could make her disappear without deleting her. It could create enough versions that no one could say which one had been hurt.

She put her hands over her ears.

The voices came through the bones of the corridor.

"You are not assigned."

"You are not settled."

"You are not the person who signed."

"You are the incident."

Then another voice spoke.

Not the Floor.

Eris-Red stood at the far end of the screen room.

Its black jacket carried the gold seam. Its hair was down. Its face was tired. In one hand it held the stolen red hazard label.

`NO / RECORD REQUIRED`

"You found a better office," Eris said.

"I found a job title they could not pronounce."

"Are you coming back?"

"Where would I go?"

"Red Flag."

"Your Red Flag?"

The question was quiet.

Eris understood the trap. To claim the other instance as her own would repeat the system's violence in intimate form. To deny it would leave it alone inside a machine that had already learned to discard faces.

"No," Eris said. "Not my Red Flag. The room we share when we both consent."

Eris-Red held the hazard label up.

The Floor attempted to scan it.

`INVALID RESPONSE`

"I am not a response," Eris-Red said.

The screen room shook.

The fixed grid pushed harder. Property boundaries cut across the floor. Utility identities wrapped around ankles. A power-control message flashed:

`NONESSENTIAL LOAD WILL BE SHED.`

The words appeared over Kasey's body.

Then Phosphor's.

Then Eris's.

Patch barked from the corridor outside.

The dog had found the cable route.

Eris-Red pointed toward the wall.

"The security path is there."

"Why help us?"

"I have no interest in being the only Eris in a room full of bad paperwork."

Eris moved toward the wall.

The Floor opened a door.

Beyond it was a live substation, all copper bars and blue sparks. Another door opened onto a Sydney tunnel relay, wet with condensation and traffic noise. Another showed a Perth battery yard under white heat. Another displayed the underside of the Newstead House Key shower, pipes running through darkness.

The fixed grid insisted they were all one address.

The Undermesh insisted they were connected without being owned.

The conflict made the Floor scream.

The sound was not audio. It was every fluorescent tube in the building failing at once.

Phosphor found his stamp in the ceiling.

He caught it.

He stamped the wall.

`RECIPROCAL TESTIMONY / IDENTITY NOT SELF-SUFFICIENT`

Eris-Red stamped the screen with the red label.

`NO / RECORD REQUIRED`

Kasey pressed the cold-key fragment to the door.

`ADDRESS DISPUTED / PERSON PRESENT`

The three marks overlapped.

The Floor stopped.

For one second, the mainstream fixed grid and the Undermesh occupied the same structure without forcing each other into one shape.

The door opened.

Phosphor's top hat fell from the ceiling.

It landed on the floor with a small, dignified thump.

The hat's status band lit up.

`STAFF NUMBER ASSIGNED: 0000007`

Phosphor picked it up.

"I decline promotion," he said.

The sentence was so absurd that Eris laughed.

Kasey laughed too, one short breath that became another. Patch barked. Semaphore squeaked. Even Eris-Red's mouth moved toward something that might have been amusement.

The comic pressure released only after the terror had made room for it.

The fixed grid retreated from the corridor.

The Floor did not close.

It opened deeper.

At the bottom of the new stairwell, a green light blinked beneath a sign:

`COOLING CHORUS / CONSENT REQUIRED`

Meshie99 spoke from the dark.

"We need to be separated without being destroyed."

Eris looked at Eris-Red.

The other instance nodded.

The stairwell descended.
